Pirkei Avot (Ethics of the Fathers)

Pirkei Avot ("Chapters of the Fathers") is a tractate of the Mishnah — the first major written compilation of rabbinic law, compiled around 200 CE. Unlike most of the Mishnah, Pirkei Avot is not legal at all. It is a collection of wisdom sayings — ethical, philosophical, sometimes paradoxical — attributed to generations of Jewish sages from roughly 300 BCE to 200 CE. These teachers argued with each other, disagreed, and left the disagreements in. The tradition of preserving multiple opinions is itself a teaching. Pirkei Avot is read in synagogues on Shabbat afternoons between Passover and the High Holidays. It belongs here because it is the intersection of Jewish intellectual tradition and universal ethical wisdom.

Ecclesiastes (Kohelet)

Ecclesiastes — in Hebrew, Kohelet, "the Assembler" or "the Preacher" — is one of the strangest books in the Bible. Written sometime in the 3rd or 2nd century BCE, it meditates on impermanence, the limits of wisdom, the pleasure and futility of earthly life, and the importance of presence. Its opening word, hevel (translated as "vanity" or "breath" or "mist"), sets the tone: all things pass. Its final counsel is to live fully anyway. The rabbis debated whether to include it in the canon at all, because its conclusions edge toward the secular. They included it. It belongs here because no text in the biblical tradition addresses deep time, impermanence, and the imperative to be present with more directness.

"Vanity of vanities! All is vanity. What profit does man have for all his labor under the sun? One generation passes away and another comes, but the earth abides forever. The sun also rises and the sun goes down, and hastens to its place where it arose. The wind goes toward the south and turns around to the north; it whirls about continually, and the wind returns again according to its circuits. All the rivers run into the sea, yet the sea is not full; to the place from which the rivers come, there they return again."
— 1:2–7
"To every thing there is a season, and a time to every purpose under the heaven: a time to be born, and a time to die; a time to plant, and a time to pluck up that which is planted; a time to kill, and a time to heal; a time to break down, and a time to build up; a time to weep, and a time to laugh; a time to mourn, and a time to dance."
— 3:1–4
"What is crooked cannot be made straight, and what is lacking cannot be numbered."
— 1:15
"There is no remembrance of former things, nor will there be any remembrance of things that are to come with those who come after."
— 1:11
"Go, eat your bread with joy, and drink your wine with a merry heart, for God has already approved what you do. Let your garments be always white. Let not oil be lacking on your head. Enjoy life with the wife whom you love, all the days of your vain life that he has given you under the sun, because that is your portion in life."
— 9:7–9
"Whatever your hand finds to do, do it with your might, for there is no work or thought or knowledge or wisdom in the grave, to which you are going."
— 9:10
