The Upanishads

The Upanishads are the philosophical heart of the Vedas — composed roughly between 800 and 200 BCE, passed down orally for centuries before that. The word itself means "sitting near," as in sitting close to a teacher. There are over two hundred, but about a dozen are considered principal. They ask the question that every contemplative tradition eventually reaches: what is the relationship between the self within and the world without? The answer the Upanishads keep circling is that this distinction dissolves under careful attention. Tat tvam asi — thou art that. The awareness looking out through your eyes is the same awareness that moves through everything.

Chandogya Upanishad

The Chandogya is one of the oldest and longest of the principal Upanishads. Its sixth chapter contains the famous teaching of Uddalaka to his son Shvetaketu — a father trying to show his child something that cannot be shown, only recognized.

"That which is the subtle essence — in that have all things their existence. That is the truth. That is the Self. And thou, Shvetaketu, art that."
— Chandogya Upanishad 6.8.7 (trans. Max Müller)
"As bees make honey by gathering the juices of many trees, and reduce the juice into one form, and the juices do not know from which tree each came — even so, all these creatures, when they have merged into Being, do not know: 'We have merged into Being.'"
— Chandogya Upanishad 6.9.1–2 (trans. Max Müller)

The image stays with you. The bees, the honey, the losing of individual origin into something shared. It is one of the earliest metaphors for interconnection in any literature.

Brihadaranyaka Upanishad

The Brihadaranyaka is the longest Upanishad and possibly the oldest piece of sustained philosophical argument in the world. Its opening prayer has been chanted for nearly three thousand years.

"From the unreal lead me to the real.
From the darkness lead me to light.
From death lead me to immortality."
— Brihadaranyaka Upanishad 1.3.28 (trans. Swami Madhavananda)
"As a spider sends forth and draws in its thread, as plants grow on the earth, as hair grows from the head and body of a living person — so from the Imperishable arises all things here."
— Brihadaranyaka Upanishad 1.1.7 (trans. Max Müller)

Isha Upanishad

The Isha is the shortest of the principal Upanishads — eighteen verses. Gandhi called it sufficient to reconstruct Hinduism if every other scripture were lost. Its opening verse asks you to see the sacred dwelling in every living thing, and to let that seeing reshape how you move through the world.

"The whole world is pervaded by the Lord. Enjoy what has been given to you; do not covet what belongs to others."
— Isha Upanishad 1 (trans. Eknath Easwaran)

Kena Upanishad

The Kena opens with a series of questions that peel away every assumption about where awareness comes from. It keeps pointing past the instrument to the one who uses it.

"What cannot be spoken with words, but that whereby words are spoken — know that alone as Brahman. What cannot be thought with the mind, but that whereby the mind thinks — know that alone as Brahman."
— Kena Upanishad 1.4–6 (trans. Eknath Easwaran)

Katha Upanishad

The Katha tells the story of Nachiketa, a young boy who journeys to the realm of Death and refuses to leave without understanding what lies beyond dying. Death tries to distract him with wealth, long life, kingdoms. Nachiketa waits. The teaching he finally receives is about the self that does not perish when the body perishes.

"The Self is not born, nor does it die. It did not spring from anything, and nothing sprang from it. Unborn, eternal, everlasting, ancient — it is not slain when the body is slain."
— Katha Upanishad 1.2.18 (trans. Eknath Easwaran)

Mundaka Upanishad

The Mundaka draws its most famous image from archery — the self as arrow, Brahman as target, the syllable Om as the bow. It asks for the kind of single-pointed attention that dissolves the boundary between archer and mark.

"Taking as a bow the great weapon of the Upanishad, one should place upon it an arrow sharpened by meditation. Stretching it with a thought directed to the essence of That, penetrate the Imperishable as the mark."
— Mundaka Upanishad 2.2.3 (trans. Robert Ernest Hume)

The Bhagavad Gita

The Gita is a 700-verse dialogue embedded in the Mahabharata — the vast Indian epic. On a battlefield, the warrior Arjuna freezes. His charioteer, Krishna (who is also God), speaks. What follows is a conversation about duty, action, devotion, and the nature of the self. The Gita has been translated more often than almost any text outside the Bible. For Arbora, it speaks most powerfully about how to act in the world without being consumed by outcomes — how to bring your full presence to what needs doing, and then let go.

"You have the right to work, but never to the fruit of work. You should never engage in action for the sake of reward, nor should you long for inaction."
— Bhagavad Gita 2.47 (trans. Eknath Easwaran)

This is the heart of karma yoga — the yoga of action. Do the work because it is yours to do. The harvest belongs to forces larger than you.

"When a man dwells on the objects of sense, he creates an attraction for them; attraction develops into desire, and desire breeds anger. From anger comes delusion; from delusion, the failure of memory; from the failure of memory, the destruction of intelligence; and from the destruction of intelligence he perishes."
— Bhagavad Gita 2.62–63 (trans. Eknath Easwaran)
"I am the same to all beings. None is hateful to me, none dear. But those who worship me with devotion — they dwell in me, and I in them."
— Bhagavad Gita 9.29 (trans. Laurie L. Patton)
"Whenever the mind, unsteady and restless, strays away — from wherever it wanders, bring it back and return it to the control of the Self."
— Bhagavad Gita 6.26 (trans. Eknath Easwaran)

Every contemplative tradition arrives at this same instruction eventually. The mind wanders. Bring it back. The practice is the returning.

Advaita Vedanta

Advaita means "not two." Vedanta means "the end (or culmination) of the Vedas." Adi Shankara, an Indian philosopher who lived around 788–820 CE, systematized this tradition — drawing together the Upanishads, the Gita, and the Brahma Sutras into a rigorous philosophical framework. His central claim: Brahman (ultimate reality) and Atman (the individual self) are identical. The apparent multiplicity of the world is maya — a kind of cognitive superimposition, the way you might mistake a rope for a snake in dim light. Shankara was a monk, a debater, a wanderer who walked the length of India. He died at thirty-two. His thought has shaped every subsequent generation of Hindu philosophy.

"Brahman alone is real. The world is appearance. The self is Brahman and nothing else."
— Adi Shankara, Vivekachudamani (trans. Swami Madhavananda)

Three sentences that contain an entire metaphysics. You can spend a lifetime unpacking them. The first is an ontological claim. The second is an epistemological one. The third is the lived consequence — the recognition that what you most deeply are is what everything most deeply is.

"Like the space in a jar, the individual soul is enclosed within names and forms. When the jar is broken, the space merges with the infinite space. So too, when ignorance is destroyed, the individual soul merges with the Supreme Self."
— Adi Shankara, Atma Bodha (trans. Swami Chinmayananda)

For Arbora, Advaita Vedanta offers one of the most uncompromising articulations of what we experience in the presence of trees, rivers, mountains — the sense that the boundary between observer and observed is thinner than we thought. The Upanishadic seers sat in forests. Their philosophy grew from that sitting, from long attention to the world as it actually presents itself when you stop narrating it.
