Cosmology

How we understand reality

Synthesis

We did not arrive here by accident, nor by design. We emerged—slowly, over billions of years—from the same processes that built mountains and carved rivers and split one species into ten thousand. We are nature observing itself.

The stories that follow come from many traditions, many peoples, many centuries. They differ in detail but converge on something essential: that reality is a web of relationships unfolding across unfathomable time. That humans are participants in this unfolding. That the trees, the stones, the rivers, and the stars are kin.

Creation Stories

Indra's Net and the Wheel of Becoming

In the Avatamsaka Sutra, the vast net of the god Indra stretches across the cosmos in every direction. At each intersection of the net hangs a jewel, and each jewel reflects every other jewel perfectly, infinitely. There is no center. There is no edge. Every single point contains the whole.

This image — composed sometime around the third century CE — anticipates what ecology and physics would later confirm: that no part of reality exists independently. The Buddhist teaching of pratityasamutpada, dependent origination, makes this explicit. Every phenomenon arises because of conditions, and those conditions arise because of other conditions, in chains that stretch beyond any single life or era. A forest fire begins with lightning, but also with drought, with wind patterns shaped by ocean temperatures, with decades of fuel accumulation. Nothing has a single cause.

Hindu cosmology deepens this with cyclical time. A kalpa — one day in the life of Brahma — lasts 4.32 billion years, strikingly close to the age of the Earth. Universes arise, unfold, dissolve, and arise again. Creation is an ongoing process, breathing in and breathing out, with no permanent beginning and no final end.

"In the heaven of Indra, there is said to be a network of pearls so arranged that if you look at one you see all the others reflected in it. In the same way, each object in the world is not merely itself but involves every other object, and in fact is everything else."
— Avatamsaka Sutra, as described by Sir Charles Eliot

The Tao and the Ten Thousand Things

The Tao Te Ching, attributed to Lao Tzu and likely composed across several centuries in ancient China, opens with a warning: whatever you are about to name, the deepest reality is beyond naming. The Tao — literally "the way" — is the source and pattern of all things, and it cannot be pinned down with words.

From this nameless source, the world emerges through differentiation. The Tao produces one. One produces two. Two produces three. Three produces the ten thousand things. This is creation as emergence: the universe unfolding from simplicity into complexity, each layer arising naturally from the one before it. There is no craftsman and no blueprint. There is only the process itself — generative, spontaneous, patient.

The Daoist concept of wu-wei — effortless action, or acting in accordance with the grain of things — follows from this cosmology. If the universe unfolds through its own inherent pattern, then the wisest response is to align with that pattern rather than to force your will against it. Water does not struggle to flow downhill. A tree does not strain to grow toward light. This is wu-wei: doing what is natural, which is often quite different from doing nothing.

"The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao. The name that can be named is not the eternal name. The nameless is the beginning of heaven and earth. The named is the mother of ten thousand things."
— Lao Tzu, Tao Te Ching, Chapter 1 (trans. Gia-Fu Feng & Jane English)
"The Tao gives birth to one. One gives birth to two. Two gives birth to three. Three gives birth to ten thousand things. The ten thousand things carry yin and embrace yang. They achieve harmony by combining these forces."
— Lao Tzu, Tao Te Ching, Chapter 42

Sky Woman and Turtle Island

In the Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) creation account, the world begins with a woman falling from the Sky World through a hole left by an uprooted tree. Below her is only water. The animals who live in that water — geese, ducks, a great turtle — see her falling and act. The geese catch her on their wings. The turtle offers its back. The muskrat dives deep, deeper than any of them has gone before, and brings up a handful of mud from the bottom. Sky Woman spreads this mud on the turtle's back, and it grows — it becomes the earth. She walks in circles on the new land, and as she walks, the earth expands. She plants the seeds she brought from the Sky World, and the world becomes green.

Creation here is collaborative. It requires the courage of the muskrat, the steadiness of the turtle, the willingness of all the animals to help someone who is falling. The earth itself is built through reciprocity — it is a gift from the animals, and Sky Woman's planting is her gift in return. Robin Wall Kimmerer, a Potawatomi botanist and member of the Citizen Potawatomi Nation, draws out the lesson: in this story, the world is given to us as a gift, and our responsibility is to care for it as the animals cared for Sky Woman.

Along the Pacific Northwest coast, the Haida and Tlingit peoples tell of Raven — the trickster who stole the sun, the moon, and the stars from an old chief who kept them locked in boxes. Raven released light into the world, transforming it from darkness into a place where things could be seen, known, and loved. Light, in these stories, is something that was shared, something that belongs to everyone.

"In the beginning was the Skyworld. And in the Skyworld there was a great tree... The woman fell and fell, through the darkness, toward the water below. But the geese saw her coming and flew up to catch her on their wings."
— Haudenosaunee oral tradition, as recounted by Robin Wall Kimmerer, Braiding Sweetgrass

The Forest That Holds Up the Sky

Among the Yanomami people of the Amazon basin, the forest is the structure of the cosmos itself. In the words of Davi Kopenawa, a Yanomami shaman and spokesperson, the forest is alive — inhabited by the xapiri, spirit beings who dance and sing in the canopy, in the rivers, in the roots of trees. When the forest is destroyed, the sky loses its support. The xapiri flee. The rains fail. The world begins to collapse inward. This is cosmology as ecology: the health of the universe depends on the health of the forest.

In the Andes, the Quechua and Aymara peoples live within a cosmos organized around ayni — sacred reciprocity. Pachamama, the earth mother, is alive and present in every field, every stone, every spring. She gives, and you give back. Before planting, before harvesting, before drinking, you pour a little on the ground — a ch'alla, an offering, an acknowledgment that what you receive was given by someone. The mountains too are alive: the apus, the mountain spirits, watch over communities and are honored with ceremonies, coca leaves, and prayer. Time in Andean cosmology is cyclical, organized around pachakuti — world-turnings, great reversals in which the old order is overturned and the cosmos renews itself.

"The white people do not dream as far as we do. They sleep a lot but only dream about themselves. Their thought remains blocked and they sleep like tapirs or tortoises. That is why they cannot understand our words."
— Davi Kopenawa, The Falling Sky: Words of a Yanomami Shaman

The Dreaming

Aboriginal Australian cosmology — the oldest continuously maintained spiritual tradition on Earth, reaching back at least 65,000 years — holds that the world was shaped during the Dreaming (Tjukurpa in Pitjantjatjara, Jukurrpa in Warlpiri, and many other names across hundreds of language groups). During the Dreaming, ancestral beings — the Rainbow Serpent, the Emu, the Honey Ant, the Seven Sisters — traveled across a formless landscape and, through their movements, their songs, their conflicts, and their resting, created every feature of the land. A ridge is where a serpent rested. A waterhole is where an ancestor struck the ground. A stand of eucalyptus marks where a ceremony was performed.

The Dreaming is not the past. It is the ongoing foundation of reality, accessible through ceremony, song, and proper relationship with country. The Songlines that crisscross the continent are both navigation routes and creation narratives — you can walk across Australia by singing the land into being, each verse corresponding to a landmark, each melody encoding thousands of years of geographical and ecological knowledge. To walk a songline is to participate in creation.

Land, in Aboriginal cosmology, is not something you own. It is something you belong to. You have obligations to your country — to maintain its sacred sites, to perform the ceremonies that keep the Dreaming alive, to pass the songs forward to the next generation intact.

"We don't own the land, the land owns us. The land is my mother, my mother is the land... Land is the starting point to where it all began. It is like picking up a piece of the earth and holding it — this is who I am, this is what I stand for."
— Traditional Aboriginal teaching, as expressed by Djinyini Gondarra

Te Kore, Te Pō, Te Ao Mārama

In Maori cosmology, creation begins in Te Kore — the void, the nothingness, the realm of potential. Te Kore is not empty. It is pregnant with possibility, the way a seed is full of a tree that has not yet grown. From Te Kore emerges Te Pō — the darkness, the night, the long gestation. Te Pō has many stages, each darker and deeper than the last, each bringing the world closer to form. And from Te Pō, finally, comes Te Ao Mārama — the world of light.

In this world of light, Ranginui (the sky father) and Papatuanuku (the earth mother) cling together in a tight embrace, and their children — the gods of forest, sea, wind, and war — are trapped between them in darkness. It is Tāne Mahuta, god of the forests and birds, who pushes his parents apart, placing his back against the earth and his feet against the sky, and slowly, with great effort, separates them. Light floods in. The world becomes inhabitable. Tāne then adorns his father with stars and his mother with plants, making beauty from separation.

In Hawaiian tradition, the Kumulipo — a creation chant of over 2,000 lines — unfolds creation in a sequence that moves from darkness to light, from sea creatures to land creatures, from plants to humans. The chant was performed at the birth of chiefs, linking each new life to the origin of the cosmos. Every living thing in the Kumulipo is genealogically related. Coral is an ancestor of humans. The taro plant is an elder sibling. Kinship, in Polynesian cosmology, extends to everything that lives.

"From the conception the increase,
From the increase the thought,
From the thought the remembrance,
From the remembrance the consciousness,
From the consciousness the desire."
— Maori cosmogonic chant, Te Kore sequence

Yggdrasil and the Well of Memory

In Norse cosmology, the universe is a tree. Yggdrasil — the world-ash — stands at the center of everything, its branches reaching into the heavens, its three roots extending down into three realms: one to Asgard, home of the gods; one to Jotunheim, home of the giants; and one to Niflheim, the world of ice and mist. Nine worlds hang in its branches. An eagle sits at the top. A dragon gnaws at the roots. A squirrel named Ratatoskr runs up and down the trunk carrying messages — and insults — between them.

Beneath one root lies Mimir's Well, the well of wisdom and memory. Odin sacrificed an eye to drink from it. Beneath another lies the Well of Urd, where the Norns — three women named Past, Present, and Becoming — tend the roots of the tree, watering it daily, because even the world-tree needs care. Even the structure of the cosmos requires maintenance, attention, and devotion.

In Celtic and Druidic traditions, trees held similar centrality. The Irish word for oak, dair, forms the root of druid — "one who knows the oak." Sacred groves were sites of worship, law, and gathering. The bile — a sacred tree, often an ancient oak or ash — stood at the center of each territory, and its destruction was an act of war against a people's spiritual identity. Trees were the axis between worlds: roots in the ancestors' realm, trunk in the human world, branches reaching toward the divine.

"I know that I hung on a windy tree,
nine full nights,
wounded with a spear, and given to Odin,
myself to myself,
on that tree of which no one knows
from what roots it runs."
— Hávamál, stanza 138, Poetic Edda

The Deep Time Narrative

4.6 billion years ago, dust and gas collapsed into a star and its attendant worlds. For a billion years, Earth was molten rock and poison atmosphere. Then—slowly, imperceptibly—chemistry became biology. Single cells divided. Oxygen accumulated. Multicellular life emerged. Forests covered the land 380 million years ago, long before any animal walked upright. The Sierra Nevada began rising 40 million years ago. Glaciers carved Yosemite Valley over the last two million years. The oldest living sequoias were seedlings when Rome was a republic.

You are the temporary expression of these ancient processes. Your atoms were forged in stars. Your DNA carries instructions refined over three billion years. When you stand among red firs at 8,000 feet and breathe in their terpenes, you are participating in a chemical conversation that predates language, predates humanity, predates thought itself.

Ein Sof and the Tree of Life

In the Kabbalistic tradition — Jewish mysticism that flowered in medieval Provence and Spain, most famously in the Zohar (composed or compiled by Moses de Leon in 13th-century Castile) — creation begins with Ein Sof, the Infinite, the Boundless. Ein Sof is beyond description, beyond even the categories of existence and non-existence. It is the fullness from which everything pours.

To make room for the world, Ein Sof withdraws. This is tzimtzum — contraction, a voluntary pulling-back of infinite light to create a space where finite things can exist. Into this space, divine light flows through ten emanations called sefirot, arranged in a pattern called the Tree of Life. Each sefirah represents a quality of reality: wisdom, understanding, lovingkindness, judgment, beauty, endurance, splendor, foundation, and presence. The Tree of Life is both a map of the cosmos and a map of the soul — the same pattern at every scale.

The Kabbalistic creation story carries within it a striking image: the vessels meant to hold the divine light shattered (shevirat ha-kelim), scattering sparks of holiness throughout the world. The human task — tikkun olam, repairing the world — is to gather these sparks, to find the sacred in the broken and scattered things of daily life. Every act of kindness, every moment of attention, is a small repair.

"When God began to create, the divine light radiated through the sefirot like water flowing through channels. But the vessels could not contain the light, and they shattered, and sparks of holiness fell into everything that exists."
— Based on Isaac Luria's account of shevirat ha-kelim, 16th century Safed

The Breath of the Compassionate

In Sufi cosmology — the mystical tradition within Islam — creation is an act of divine self-disclosure. The great Andalusian mystic Ibn Arabi (1165–1240) taught wahdat al-wujud, the unity of being: that everything in existence is a manifestation of a single divine reality, the way sunlight passing through a prism becomes a thousand colors while remaining one light. The multiplicity of the world is real, but it arises from an underlying oneness.

The cosmos, in this view, comes into being through the Nafas al-Rahman — the Breath of the Compassionate. God "breathes out" existence the way a person exhales mist on a cold morning, and each being is a word spoken in that breath. Creation is continuous: the world is being breathed into existence in every moment, sustained by an act of divine generosity that never pauses. To exist is to be loved into being.

Rumi, the 13th-century Persian poet and Sufi master, returned to these themes again and again. For Rumi, the human being is a guest house, visited daily by different emotions and experiences, each one sent as a guide. The universe is alive with meaning, if you have the eyes to read it. Love — not abstract or sentimental, but fierce, demanding, and transformative — is the force that moves the stars and draws the soul toward its source.

"I am not this hair, I am not this skin, I am the soul that lives within. I have lived on the lip of insanity, wanting to know reasons, knocking on a door. It opens. I have been knocking from the inside."
— Rumi, trans. Coleman Barks
"I was a hidden treasure and I loved to be known, so I created the world that I might be known."
— Hadith qudsi, widely cited in Sufi tradition

The Book of Creation

Within the Christian mystical tradition, a current runs alongside and sometimes against the institutional church — a current that sees the natural world as the primary scripture, as a revelation as profound as any written text. Francis of Assisi (1181–1226) addressed the sun as brother, the moon as sister, the wind, water, fire, and earth as family. His Canticle of the Creatures, composed near the end of his life while nearly blind and in great pain, praises the living world with the intimacy of someone who has spent years paying attention to it. Francis did not love nature as an idea. He loved particular things: a particular wolf, a particular leper, the particular cold of a stone floor at dawn.

Meister Eckhart (c. 1260–1328), a Dominican friar and one of the most radical theologians in Christian history, pushed further. For Eckhart, the divine is not a being among beings. It is the ground of being itself — the silent, generative darkness from which all things emerge and to which they return. "God is creating the entire world, fully and totally, in this present now," Eckhart taught. Every moment is creation. Every leaf unfolding is the first day. The mystic's task is to become empty enough to notice what is already happening.

Thomas Berry, a 20th-century Catholic priest and self-described "geologian," carried this tradition forward. Berry argued that the universe itself is the primary sacred community, and that the human story only makes sense as a chapter within the larger story of the cosmos. His work laid the groundwork for what is now called creation spirituality — a theology grounded in wonder, ecology, and deep time.

"Praised be You, my Lord, through Sister Mother Earth, who sustains and governs us, and who produces various fruits with colored flowers and herbs."
— Francis of Assisi, Canticle of the Creatures, c. 1225
"God is creating the entire world, fully and totally, in this present now. Everything God created six thousand years ago — and even previous to that — God creates now all at once."
— Meister Eckhart, Sermon 10

On the Nature of Reality

Non-Duality

Across traditions — Advaita Vedanta, Zen Buddhism, Sufism, Kabbalah, Meister Eckhart's mysticism — a common insight keeps surfacing: the separation between self and world, between observer and observed, between "in here" and "out there" is not as solid as it appears. The Upanishads state it plainly: Tat tvam asi — "You are that." The consciousness looking out through your eyes is made of the same substance as the thing it sees. The boundary between you and the red fir you are leaning against at 8,000 feet is real at one level — bark, skin, different cellular structures — and illusory at another. You share carbon. You exchange gases. You are both temporary patterns in the same flow of matter and energy.

This is not a claim that differences do not matter or that everything is "really" the same. It is a claim about what is most fundamental. Beneath the real and important diversity of forms, there is a shared ground. The wave is distinct from other waves, but it is never separate from the ocean.

"The eye through which I see God is the same eye through which God sees me; my eye and God's eye are one eye, one seeing, one knowing, one love."
— Meister Eckhart, Sermon 12

Process Over Substance

Western philosophy has often asked: what are things made of? What is the substance that underlies change? But the traditions Arbora draws from tend to ask a different question: what is happening? What is the process?

The Buddhist teaching of anicca — impermanence — holds that nothing is fixed. Everything is in the process of becoming something else. A mountain is not a thing; it is an event, happening at a pace too slow for human eyes to follow. A river is not an object; it is a verb, a continuous act of flowing. The pre-Socratic philosopher Heraclitus, writing in Ephesus around 500 BCE, arrived at the same insight: panta rhei, everything flows. You cannot step into the same river twice, because the river is always different and so are you.

Daoist philosophy deepens this with the concept of perpetual transformation. The Zhuangzi is full of metamorphoses: a butterfly dreaming it is a man, a fish becoming a bird, a tree that survives because it is "useless." These are not fantasies. They are reminders that identities are provisional, boundaries are porous, and the universe is always in motion.

"No man ever steps in the same river twice, for it is not the same river, and he is not the same man."
— Heraclitus, Fragment 12

Change as Fundamental

If process precedes substance, then change is the most basic feature of reality. The seasons turn. Cells divide and die. Stars burn through their fuel and collapse. Mountain ranges rise and erode to plains over hundreds of millions of years. The Buddhist wheel of samsara turns. The Hindu yugas cycle. The Andean pachakuti overturns the old order.

Modern science confirms this at every scale. At the quantum level, particles flicker in and out of existence. At the cosmic level, the universe is expanding, cooling, and evolving. The Earth's magnetic poles wander and reverse. Continents drift. Species emerge, flourish, and disappear — 99% of all species that have ever lived are now extinct. To resist change is to resist the most fundamental quality of the world you live in.

The Stoics understood this clearly. Marcus Aurelius, writing in a military camp on the Danube frontier, returned to impermanence constantly: the fame of Alexander is dust, the power of Rome will pass, your own body is borrowed material. This is not nihilism. It is an invitation to participate fully in the present, knowing that the present is the only form in which reality is ever available.

"All conditioned things are impermanent. When one sees this with wisdom, one turns away from suffering."
— Dhammapada, verse 277

Consciousness

What is awareness? The traditions Arbora draws from do not agree on a single answer, but they share a conviction that consciousness is more fundamental than modern materialism typically assumes. In the Vedantic tradition, consciousness (Brahman) is the ground of all reality — matter arises within it. In Buddhism, mind is central to the construction of experience: "With our thoughts we make the world," says the Dhammapada. In Aboriginal Australian cosmology, the land is conscious — country knows you, recognizes you, responds to your presence and your songs.

Contemporary science has begun to acknowledge the difficulty of the problem. David Chalmers' "hard problem of consciousness" — why does subjective experience exist at all? — remains unsolved. The philosopher Thomas Nagel asked what it is like to be a bat, and the question has never been adequately answered. Meanwhile, forest ecologists like Suzanne Simard have documented decision-making behavior in trees: mother trees recognize their own seedlings and feed them preferentially through mycorrhizal networks. Whether this constitutes consciousness depends on your definition, but it is certainly a form of responsiveness, of awareness, of something that looks a great deal like care.

Arbora does not require a position on the metaphysics of consciousness. It asks only that you take seriously the possibility that awareness extends beyond the human skull — that the world you move through may be, in some sense, looking back.

Interconnection

This is the thread that runs through everything above. Indra's Net. Dependent origination. The Tao producing the ten thousand things. Pachamama and ayni. The Songlines connecting every feature of the Australian continent. The sefirot emanating from Ein Sof. The Breath of the Compassionate sustaining all beings. Yggdrasil holding nine worlds in its branches. Mycorrhizal networks connecting a forest underground.

Every tradition represented here arrives at the same structural insight: reality is relational. Nothing exists in isolation. The tree needs the fungus, the fungus needs the tree, both need the rain, the rain needs the ocean, the ocean needs the rivers, the rivers need the mountains, the mountains need the tectonic forces deep in the Earth. Pull one thread and the whole fabric moves.

Ecology calls this a system. Buddhism calls it dependent origination. Indigenous traditions worldwide call it kinship. Physics calls it fields and forces. Whatever the language, the insight is the same: you are embedded in a web of relationships that extends in every direction — backward through time to the first cells and the first stars, outward through space to the mycorrhizal network beneath your feet and the atmosphere above your head, forward to the beings who will inherit whatever you leave behind.

To know this is the beginning. To live as though it were true is the practice.
