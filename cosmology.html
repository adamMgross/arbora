Cosmology

How we understand reality

This section is under development. It will contain a synthesis preamble, multiple creation stories from aligned traditions, and philosophical statements on the nature of reality.

Synthesis

We did not arrive here by accident, nor by design. We emerged—slowly, over billions of years—from the same processes that built mountains and carved rivers and split one species into ten thousand. We are not observers of nature. We are nature observing itself.

The stories that follow come from many traditions, many peoples, many centuries. They differ in detail but converge on something essential: that reality is not a collection of separate things but a web of relationships unfolding across unfathomable time. That humans are participants in this unfolding, not its purpose. That the trees, the stones, the rivers, and the stars are not backdrop—they are kin.

The Deep Time Narrative

4.6 billion years ago, dust and gas collapsed into a star and its attendant worlds. For a billion years, Earth was molten rock and poison atmosphere. Then—slowly, imperceptibly—chemistry became biology. Single cells divided. Oxygen accumulated. Multicellular life emerged. Forests covered the land 380 million years ago, long before any animal walked upright. The Sierra Nevada began rising 40 million years ago. Glaciers carved Yosemite Valley over the last two million years. The oldest living sequoias were seedlings when Rome was a republic.

You are the temporary expression of these ancient processes. Your atoms were forged in stars. Your DNA carries instructions refined over three billion years. When you stand among red firs at 8,000 feet and breathe in their terpenes, you are participating in a chemical conversation that predates language, predates humanity, predates thought itself.
