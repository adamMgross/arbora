Rituals

Seasonal observances & community gatherings

Rituals are tied to natural cycles and designed for real life. They are loose frameworks. Adapt them. Skip them when you need to. Return to them when you are ready. The practice is in the returning.

The Seasons

Spring — Renewal & Growth

March through May. The world wakes up. First camping trips. Wildflowers in the foothills. Birds return. Trees bloom on every street. This is the season of emergence and beginning again.

Opening invocation: The earth is waking, and so may I. What was dormant in me through the winter — what I set down, what I let rest — let it emerge now if it is ready. I do not force growth. I make conditions for it.

Practices for this season:

  • Walk a new route through your neighborhood. Notice what is blooming that was not blooming last month.
  • Plant something — a seed in a pot on the porch, herbs in a window box. Tend it through the season. Watch it struggle or thrive.
  • Clean a space in your home that has accumulated. Spring cleaning is a physical enactment of renewal. Let go of what you no longer need.

Readings: Kimmerer's chapters on planting and maple sugar in Braiding Sweetgrass. The Passover Haggadah's themes of liberation and new beginnings (read as metaphor). Mary Oliver's spring poems.

Summer — Abundance & Pilgrimage

June through August. Wild places open. Backpacking season. Long days, abundant light, community gathering. This is the season of immersion and fullness.

Opening invocation: The light is long and the mountains are open. This is the season of abundance — of warmth, of time, of the wild places that restore me. Let me not waste the fullness of these days. Let me go where the trees are, and let them change me.

Practices for this season:

  • Make at least one trip to your wild place as pilgrimage (see below).
  • Host an outdoor gathering: dinner in the yard, a hike with friends, an evening on someone's porch with wine and no agenda.
  • Read or listen to nature writing on the trail. McPhee, Muir, Snyder. Let the landscape and the text illuminate each other.

Readings: Muir's My First Summer in the Sierra. McPhee's Assembling California. Gary Snyder's poetry. The solstice passages from any tradition that honors the longest day.

Autumn — Gratitude & Letting Go

September through November. Return to urban rhythm. Days shorten. Leaves turn where they can. The High Holidays come. This is the season of gratitude, reflection, and release.

Opening invocation: The year is turning. The light retreats. What did I harvest from the long days — what did I learn, what did I carry home from the mountains? Let me hold it lightly. Let me release what the season is releasing. Gratitude for what was. Openness to what comes.

Practices for this season:

  • Write a brief accounting: what did this year give you? What did you give back? This is a cheshbon ha-nefesh, an accounting of the soul, adapted from the Jewish tradition of the Days of Awe.
  • Give something away: a book you loved to someone who would love it, a meal to a neighbor, a donation to a cause that aligns with the principles.
  • If you have a tradition that marks the turn of the year — High Holidays, Samhain, Diwali, the autumn equinox — observe it. The ancestors showed up. So do you.

Readings: The Book of Ecclesiastes (Kohelet) — "To everything there is a season." Buddhist texts on impermanence. Wendell Berry's autumn poems. Boyd Varty on loss and renewal.

Winter — Rest & Deep Time

December through February. Dormancy. Snow in the mountains. Long nights for reading and inner work. This is the season of rest, ancestors, and the deep dark.

Opening invocation: The world is resting, and I may rest too. The longest night is not emptiness — it is depth. The roots are working beneath the frozen ground. My ancestors survived winters far harder than this. I carry their endurance. In the darkness, I turn inward. In the stillness, I listen.

Practices for this season:

  • Read more, screen less. Winter is the reading season. Work through one of the foundational texts from the canon — Meditations, the Tao Te Ching, Braiding Sweetgrass if you haven't revisited it.
  • Light candles on the darkest evenings. This is borrowed from everywhere — Hanukkah, Yule, Dongzhi, Advent — because the instinct is universal: when it is dark, make light.
  • Tell ancestor stories. At dinner, on a ski trip, around a fire. Name the people who came before you. Where they were from, what they endured, what they loved.

Readings: Marcus Aurelius' Meditations (winter is its natural season). The Kabbalistic creation narrative — emanation from darkness into light. Wohlleben's chapters on trees in winter. Norse mythology — Yggdrasil endures the long dark.

Community Gatherings

The Weekly Gathering

Inspired by Shabbat but freed from theistic framing. A weekly or bi-weekly evening — Friday is traditional, but any evening works — dedicated to presence with people you love. This is the most important ritual in Arbora. Everything else is individual practice. This is communal.

Before

Prepare food with intention. It does not need to be elaborate — bread, wine, a one-pot meal — but it should be made by hand. Set the table. Light candles. Put all phones in a drawer or another room. The preparation is part of the ritual.

Opening

Light the candles. Whoever lights them may say:

We light these candles to mark this time as different from ordinary time. For the next few hours, we are not producing, not consuming, not performing. We are here, together, and that is enough.

A moment of silence — ten seconds, thirty seconds, whatever the group can hold. Just a collective breath. A transition from the noise of the week to the quiet of the table.

An optional opening invocation, rotating weekly:

We gather not because we must, but because the alternative — isolation dressed up as independence — is a lie our culture tells. We are social animals. We evolved around fire. This table is our fire tonight.

The Meal

Before eating, a brief acknowledgment — adapted from the Jewish hamotzi, the Buddhist meal verse, and indigenous gratitude practices:

We give thanks for this food — for the soil that grew it, the rain that fed it, the hands that prepared it, and the company that makes it more than fuel. We eat not alone but together, and in doing so we remember that eating is an act of connection to the living world.

If there is wine or drink:

This cup holds the work of sun and rain and time. We share it as people have shared wine and water and tea for thousands of years — not because it is sacred in itself, but because the sharing is.

Eat. Talk. Laugh. Argue. There is no script for the meal itself. The absence of screens is the only rule.

Conversation

If the table wants structure, choose one prompt. If conversation flows on its own, skip them entirely. These are kindling, not the fire.

  • "What did you notice this week that you might have otherwise missed?"
  • "What ancestor or tradition are you carrying forward — knowingly or not?"
  • "What are you questioning right now? What certainty has started to crack?"
  • "What did you receive this week that you did not earn?"

Closing

When the evening winds down naturally — do not force an ending — someone may offer a brief closing:

We came to this table from separate days and separate weeks. We leave it having been, for a few hours, part of something older than any of us — the ancient human practice of gathering, feeding one another, and speaking honestly in the firelight. Go gently. Come back soon.

Optional: read a short passage from the canon together — a poem, a paragraph from Meditations, a verse from the Tao Te Ching. Let the text be a shared departing gift.

Feast Days

Three to four times a year, tied to the turning of the seasons. Larger than the weekly gathering — invite more people, cook more food, go outside if you can. These are the high holidays of Arbora.

Spring Equinox — around March 20

The return of balanced light. A celebration of emergence, planting, beginning again.

The light and dark are equal today. From here, the days grow longer. Whatever we plant now — in soil, in habit, in relationship — has the whole growing season ahead of it. Let us begin.

Format: An outdoor element if possible — eat outside, walk before the meal. Potluck. Each guest brings a seed or a cutting to trade. Read Kimmerer on planting. Conversation prompt: "What are you beginning?"

Summer Solstice — around June 21

The longest day. Abundance, light, the opening of pilgrimage season.

This is the most light we will receive. The mountains are open, the rivers are running, the world is as alive as it will be all year. Let us not waste the longest day. Let us fill it with the people and the places that matter.

Format: Eat outdoors at sunset. Fire pit or candles after dark. Share stories of places that have changed you. Plan a trip together — a hike, a camping weekend. Read Muir or Snyder. Stay up late.

Autumn Equinox — around September 22

The return of balanced light, now tilting toward dark. Harvest, gratitude, release.

The year has given abundantly. The days grow shorter now, and what remains of the light is precious. Let us gather what we have harvested — in knowledge, in friendship, in experience — and let go of what we no longer need to carry.

Format: A harvest meal — dishes made from seasonal, local ingredients. Each person names something they are grateful for and something they are releasing. Read Ecclesiastes 3 ("To everything there is a season") or Buddhist impermanence texts. Light a candle for someone who is gone.

Winter Solstice — around December 21

The longest night. Rest, ancestors, light in the darkness.

This is the deepest dark. From tomorrow, the light returns — imperceptibly at first, then undeniably. Our ancestors marked this night because they understood: the dark is not the enemy. It is the rest before renewal. We light our candles not to banish the night but to honor it.

Format: Candles — as many as you want. A warm, heavy meal. Tell ancestor stories around the table. Each person lights a candle and names someone who came before them. Read the Kabbalistic creation narrative (light emanating from Ein Sof) or the Yggdrasil story (the tree enduring through Ragnarok). Gifts optional — if given, let them be books, food, or handmade things.

Rites of Passage

Most traditions have ceremonies for the threshold moments of a life — birth, adulthood, marriage, death. Arbora offers five loose templates, non-theistic but grounded in the oldest language for ceremony: witness, fire, community, words spoken aloud. Each is a structure, not a script. Adapt them as freely as the occasion asks.

Welcoming a New Life

For a birth, an adoption, or any arrival of a new person into your community's circle of care. The ceremony asks the community to extend its protection and attention to someone who has not yet asked for anything.

You have arrived from somewhere none of us remember. You come with no language yet, no categories, no history of harm. You are the newest link in a chain that extends back through everyone in this room, back through their parents and theirs, back to the first humans who looked at the night sky and wondered, back further than that. We receive you. We offer you our names and our stories. You will make of them what only you can.

Format: Each person present names themselves to the child — who they are, how they are connected, what they hope to give. Light a candle. Read from Kimmerer's passage on the gift of the first strawberry, or the Polynesian Kumulipo on genealogy extending back to the first creatures. No special objects required — the presence of the people is the ceremony.

Coming of Age

This is not tied to a fixed age. It is the moment you notice you have become a different kind of person — that you are carrying your life rather than being carried by it. It may come at sixteen or at thirty-two. The ceremony honors the leaving behind as much as the arriving.

Something is ending. The person you were before this threshold — their confusions, their dependencies, their way of being in the world — deserves a respectful farewell. What are you leaving behind? Name it. Now: what are you carrying forward? Name that too. The people in this room have watched you become who you are. They will continue watching. That is what a community is for.

Format: A question addressed to the person: what are you leaving behind, and what are you choosing to carry forward? A gift of a book from the canon, chosen for this specific person. A meal they choose. Optionally: each person present says one sentence about what they have seen grow in the person being honored.

Marriage and Partnership

For the commitment to make your life a joint project with another person. The ceremony is grounded in witness, earth, and reciprocity — the oldest language for what commitment means.

You are choosing to make your life a joint project. Not to merge into one person — you will each remain yourselves, which is what makes the partnership worth having — but to say: whatever I encounter, I will encounter with you beside me. In abundance and in difficulty. In the years when everything is clear and in the years when nothing is. The people in this room are your witnesses. They will remember this, even when you forget.

Format: Candles from both families or households, lit and combined. Bread broken and shared. A reading chosen by the couple from the canon — something that captures what they want their life together to be oriented toward. Three promises, framed in the spirit of Arbora's principles: to see each other clearly, to remain curious about each other, and to tend the partnership as a living thing. The witnesses say: we remember this.

Honoring the Dead

Adapted from the yizkor tradition: the act of naming the dead, telling a story, keeping them present in the community's memory. This is not a eulogy. It is a practice of sustained attention to someone who is gone.

The dead are not absent. They are present in the specific way that the past is always present — in what we carry, in what we do because of them, in the choices they made that made our lives possible. We name them because naming is how we keep them in the living world a little longer. We tell their stories because stories are the only immortality any of us actually get.

Format: Go around the table or the room. Each person names the person being honored — not a eulogy, but a specific memory. A fact. A smell. Something they said once that stayed. Light a candle. Optional readings: Primo Levi's epigraph from If This Is a Man ("Engrave them on your hearts"); Czesław Miłosz's "Dedication"; Yehuda Amichai's "The Place Where We Are Right." Eat together afterward — the meal is part of the ceremony.

Major Life Transition

For the transitions that have no traditional name: a move, a career change, a loss that changes everything, a recovery, an identity shift. These are the passages most traditions ignore, and they deserve ceremony as much as births and deaths do.

Every year the trees release their leaves. This is not failure — it is how trees conserve what matters and let go of what they no longer need to carry. Something is ending in your life. It served you. It may have cost you. It brought you here. Give it a respectful farewell. Then turn and face what is beginning. The people in this room will walk with you across the threshold.

Format: Two parts. First, acknowledge what is ending: a brief toast or a few spoken words that name the chapter that is closing and what it meant. Second, acknowledge what is beginning: name, as specifically as you can, what you are moving toward. One reading from the canon, chosen for the particular transition. The witnesses respond: we see you crossing. We will remember where you came from.

Pilgrimage

The annual return to the Sierra Nevada — or whatever wild place calls to you — is pilgrimage. The difference is intention.

What Makes It Pilgrimage

A pilgrimage is a journey toward something — a place, a quality of attention, a confrontation with scale and silence that ordinary life cannot provide. You go to the mountains to remember what your life is part of.

Three things distinguish pilgrimage from recreation: intention (you go with purpose), attention (you practice noticing — species, geological features, weather, your own responses), and gratitude (you acknowledge that you are a guest in a place that does not need you).

Before Departure

I am going to the mountains — not to conquer them, not to check a box, but to be in the presence of something older and larger than myself. I go as a student, a guest, a temporary participant in systems that were here long before me and will endure long after. Let me be worthy of the silence I am about to enter.

On the Trail

Daily practices while in the backcountry:

  • Morning: Before breaking camp, stand still for one minute. Face the valley or the ridge. Do not photograph. Just see.
  • Walking: Spend at least one hour per day without speaking. Let the trail sounds — wind, water, birdsong, your own footfalls — be the only input.
  • Evening: Name three things you noticed today that you have never noticed before. A lichen pattern. A tree species at a new elevation. The way the light hit a particular granite face. Write them down if you want to, but the naming is the practice.
  • Gratitude: When you arrive at camp, when you filter water, when you eat — acknowledge. The water is a gift. The trail is a gift. The red fir you lean against at 8,000 feet is a gift. Kimmerer's Honorable Harvest applies here above all: take only what you need, leave more than you found.

Returning Home

The hardest part of pilgrimage is not the climb. It is the re-entry — the return to noise, to screens, to pavement. The spiritual bipolarity between the mountains and the city is real, and there is no shortcut through it. But there is a practice:

I have come home from the mountains. What they gave me — silence, scale, the company of trees — does not vanish because I left. I carry the Sierra in my body. The red fir is in my lungs. The granite is in my sense of time. Let me not lose this too quickly. Let me bring some fraction of the mountain stillness into the city days.

In the first week after returning: sit on the porch each morning in silence, longer than usual. Revisit a photo or a journal entry from the trip — to extend the attention you practiced there into the life you live here. Notice the trees in your neighborhood with the same eyes you used at elevation. They are different species, but they are no less alive.
