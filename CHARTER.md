## Part 0: How to Use This Document

This document is the output of a deep conversation between Adam and Claude in April 2026. It captures Adam's spiritual background, values, and vision for building a syncretic personal religion. It is designed to be handed to future Claude agents (or collaborators) who will:

1. **Expand** specific branches of the tree structure with curated texts, prayers, stories, and practices
2. **Iterate** based on Adam's feedback after experimentation
3. **Discover** new traditions and sources that align with the core values

**Key Design Principles for Future Agents:**

- **Curate, don't invent.** Pull directly from existing traditions. Do not rewrite wisdom from scratch when it already exists beautifully elsewhere. Humility over hubris.
- **The canon is never closed.** Any tradition from any culture or time period that aligns with the core values is welcome. Never assume the list of sources is complete.
- **But the canon must be lean.** Not everything that's relevant belongs in the core text. Think of this like Judaism: there's the Torah you read every week, the prayer book you use on Shabbat, the poetry and psalms you dip into, and then the vast library you could spend a lifetime exploring. The core canon should be something a person can actually revisit regularly without feeling burdened. Broader material goes in reference tiers, not the main text. Use the three-tier system (see Part 6) to decide what gets full treatment vs. a pointer.
- **Tree structure is sacred.** All content should be organized within the tree. Each node should contain enough parent context that a subtree can be regenerated independently.
- **Multiple genres, not one voice.** The religion contains Torah-style narrative, Upanishad-style philosophy, Tao Te Ching-style aphorism, prayer, poetry, and ethical teaching. Each genre serves a different purpose. Do not flatten them into one tone.
- **Loose practices, not rigid prescriptions.** Daily and seasonal practices should be suggestive and invocational, not legalistic. Leave room for personal adaptation.
- **Community and gathering are as important as contemplation.** Do not let the intellectual and solitary dimensions overshadow the relational and communal ones.
- **Use the abstract definition (Part 2) as a diagnostic tool.** When evaluating whether to add something, ask: which of the eight religious functions does this serve? If it doesn't clearly serve one, it's probably bulk, not substance.
- **Mobile-first design.** Adam interacts with the site primarily on his phone—during commutes, on the porch, in the car. All content must be readable and navigable on a phone screen. Touch targets must be adequate (44px minimum). Typography must be legible at mobile sizes. The tree page should scroll horizontally if needed.

## Part 2: What Is a Religion? (Abstract Definition)

Before designing our specific religion, we need a working model of what religions *are* and what they *do* for humans. This serves as our abstract base class—the structural blueprint that any religion implements, and that ours must consciously fill in.

### What a Religion Provides (Core Functions)

At their most fundamental, religions serve these functions for their adherents:

1. **Cosmology**: An account of what reality is, where it came from, and how it works. Answers the question: *What is all of this?*
2. **Orientation**: A sense of the individual's place within that reality. Answers: *Where do I fit?*
3. **Ethics**: A framework for how to live, what to value, and how to treat others. Answers: *How should I act?*
4. **Practice**: Disciplines, rituals, and habits that cultivate the religion's values in daily life. Answers: *What do I do?*
5. **Community**: Structures for gathering, belonging, mutual support, and shared identity. Answers: *Who are my people?*
6. **Meaning-Making**: Narrative, symbol, and myth that give purpose and coherence to suffering, joy, death, and change. Answers: *Why does any of this matter?*
7. **Aesthetic / Sensory Experience**: Music, art, architecture, food, scent, and embodied ritual that make the religion *felt*, not just thought. Answers: *What does this feel like?*
8. **Temporal Structure**: Calendars, seasons, holidays, and life-cycle rituals that organize time around sacred rhythms rather than purely economic ones. Answers: *What time is it, really?*

### Common Types / Clusters of Religions

Religions tend to cluster around different emphases:

- **Theistic / Devotional**: Centered on relationship with a deity or deities. Strong on community, narrative, and temporal structure. (Judaism, Christianity, Islam, Hinduism in devotional forms)
- **Philosophical / Ethical**: Centered on how to live well and think clearly. Strong on ethics and practice. (Stoicism, Confucianism, Buddhism in some forms, Epicureanism)
- **Mystical / Experiential**: Centered on direct experience of ultimate reality. Strong on practice and meaning-making. (Sufism, Zen, Kabbalah, Christian mysticism, Advaita Vedanta)
- **Animist / Land-Based**: Centered on relationship with the living world—land, plants, animals, ancestors, spirits. Strong on cosmology, practice, and temporal structure. (Indigenous traditions worldwide, Shinto, Druidic traditions)
- **Syncretic / Integrative**: Consciously draws from multiple traditions. Strong on intellectual rigor and curation. (Bahá'í, Unitarian Universalism, various New Religious Movements, and—what we are building here)

### Design Implications for Our Religion

Our religion should consciously implement *all eight functions* listed above. When we identify gaps (e.g., weak on aesthetic/sensory experience, or weak on temporal structure), we know where to focus development effort.

Our religion draws most heavily from the **Philosophical/Ethical**, **Mystical/Experiential**, and **Animist/Land-Based** clusters, while incorporating the **community and temporal structures** that theistic religions do exceptionally well (e.g., Shabbat, seasonal holidays) without requiring theistic belief.

The classical virtue traditions (Greek, Roman, Renaissance) primarily serve the **Ethics** and **Orientation** functions. Buddhism and Daoism primarily serve **Cosmology**, **Practice**, and **Meaning-Making**. Indigenous traditions primarily serve **Cosmology**, **Temporal Structure**, and **Aesthetic/Sensory Experience**. Judaism primarily serves **Community**, **Temporal Structure**, **Ancestral Identity**, and **Intellectual Rigor**.

This framework should be used by future agents to evaluate whether proposed additions actually fill a needed function or just add bulk.

## Part 3: What We Are Building

### The Vision

A **syncretic, non-theistic religion** that:

- Draws from all human wisdom traditions that align with its core values
- Is structured as a **tree** (both symbolically and literally as a data structure)
- Has a full **canon** of texts in multiple genres (narrative, philosophy, aphorism, prayer, poetry, ethical teaching)
- Includes **daily practices** that are loose and invocational rather than rigid
- Includes **seasonal and community rituals** (including an adapted Shabbat-like weekly gathering)
- Is designed to be **iterated upon** through experimentation and feedback (scientific method approach)
- Is initially guinea-pigged by Adam but designed to be **universally applicable** to others with similar sensibilities
- Is **never closed** — always open to new traditions, texts, and practices that align with its values

### Design Metaphor: The Tree

The religion is organized as a **tree data structure** for several reasons:

1. **Symbolic resonance**: Trees are central to Adam's spirituality. The religion's structure mirrors what it reveres.
2. **Fractal engagement**: You can engage at the root level (core values), branch level (major domains), or leaf level (specific practices/texts). Different depths for different moments.
3. **Scalability**: New branches and leaves can be added without restructuring the whole.
4. **Computational elegance**: Each subtree can be regenerated independently by providing parent context + a new directive.
5. **Artistic potential**: The tree structure lends itself to visual representations, diagrams, and artistic renderings of the religion itself.

## Part 4: Core Values (The Root)

These eight values are the root system of the tree. Everything else grows from them.

1. **Interconnection across time and beyond humanity** — All things are related. Humans are embedded in ecological, temporal, and ancestral systems, not separate from them.
2. **Reverence for deep time and natural systems** — Awe for geological processes, evolution, forests, mountains, the slow work of millions of years.
3. **Ancestral consciousness and lineage** — Honoring those who came before. Carrying forward their stories, their survival, their wisdom.
4. **Presence and clarity of mind** — Silence, meditation, embodied awareness. Freedom from constant input and distraction.
5. **Ethical action rooted in reciprocity** — Not commandment from above, but mutual obligation. What you take, you give back.
6. **Intellectual rigor and perpetual questioning** — Study, debate, curiosity. Never accepting simplified narratives. Seeking truth even when uncomfortable.
7. **Community, gathering, and shared practice** — Breaking bread together, rituals with others, human connection as sacred. Hospitality and presence with loved ones.
8. **Wonder and humble participation** — Recognition that we are small. Openness to mystery and the unknowable. Participation in something larger than oneself.

**Classical Philosophy — Expanded Breakdown:**

The Greco-Roman philosophical tradition is not one thing. The most aligned schools, in priority order:

- **Stoicism**: Virtue as the highest good, living according to nature, what's within your control, cosmic perspective. *Key texts*: Marcus Aurelius' *Meditations*, Epictetus' *Discourses*, Seneca's *Letters*. The most directly actionable classical tradition for daily practice.
- **Aristotelian Virtue Ethics**: The idea that excellence (arete) is cultivated through habit and practice, not just belief. The concept of eudaimonia (flourishing) as the aim of life. The golden mean. Deeply relevant to how we think about practice and character.
- **Epicureanism**: Widely misunderstood—not hedonism, but the pursuit of simple pleasures, friendship, philosophical contemplation, and freedom from anxiety (ataraxia). Aligned with our emphasis on presence, community, and modest living.
- **Pre-Socratics**: Heraclitus (everything flows, change is fundamental), Empedocles (elements and forces), Thales and the Milesian naturalists. The earliest Western attempts to understand nature without mythology—directly relevant to our cosmology.
- **Neoplatonism**: The emanation model (the One flowing outward into multiplicity) has parallels with Kabbalistic emanation and Buddhist interconnection. Plotinus as a bridge between Greek thought and mystical traditions.
- **Cynicism**: Radical simplicity, living according to nature, rejecting social convention. Diogenes as the extreme version of what Adam does mildly when he goes solo backpacking.

*Further exploration (not core, but worth noting)*: Renaissance humanism as the revival of these traditions; Roman Republican virtue (Cicero, Cato) and its influence on American founding thought; Pyrrhonian skepticism and its relationship to Buddhist epistemology.

## Part 7: The Eight Core Principles (Draft)

**Number Significance**: Eight was chosen for its resonance across traditions (Buddhist Eightfold Path, I Ching's eight trigrams, eight directions in many indigenous cosmologies) and its mathematical elegance (2³, clean binary).

### The Eight Principles

1. **Honor the Web of Life** — All beings are interconnected. Your actions ripple outward through systems you cannot see.

2. **Dwell in Deep Time** — You are a temporary expression of ancient processes. Think in scales of mountains and forests, not news cycles.

3. **Seek Clarity Through Practice** — Meditation, movement, silence, and study sharpen your perception and reduce your suffering.

4. **Give and Receive in Reciprocity** — Nothing is owed; everything is a gift. Take only what you need. Give back more than you take.

5. **Remember Your Ancestors** — You carry forward the survival, wisdom, and stories of those who came before. Honor them by living fully.

6. **Gather and Break Bread** — Community, hospitality, and presence with others are sacred. Show up for your people.

7. **Question Everything; Revere the Search** — Study, debate, and intellectual honesty are spiritual acts. Never accept simplified narratives.

8. **Stand Humbly Before the Unknowable** — You are small. Mystery is not a problem to solve but a reality to inhabit. Wonder is enough.
