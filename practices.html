Daily Practices

Loose invocations, adapted freely

Practices are suggestive frameworks. Adapt them to your life. The goal is to bring the qualities of presence, interconnection, and reverence into ordinary hours without adding another obligation to an already full day.

Morning

The Porch Ritual

Sit with your tea, your people, your animals. Face the sun. Leave your phone inside. Let your mind be where your body is. This is already sacred — you are simply acknowledging it.

The Morning Cup

Tea has been a contemplative practice for as long as humans have grown it — which is to say for at least three thousand years. The Chinese gongfu cha tradition teaches that tea preparation is inseparable from tea drinking: the careful warming of the pot, the precise temperature of the water, the small cups that force you to drink slowly and refill often. The Japanese chado (the Way of Tea) built an entire aesthetic philosophy around a single bowl of powdered tea — wa, kei, sei, jaku: harmony, respect, purity, tranquility. The tea room is a space where rank falls away and attention is the only currency.

The Yunnan black tea on your porch came from a mountainside on the other side of the world, from soil and rain and hands you will never know, through a chain of cultivation and trade that is thousands of years old. The warmth in your palms is the warmth of that soil, that rain, those hands. You are holding the world in a cup.

But the deeper practice is simpler: the cup is one thing. You are with it. You are not planning the day, not reviewing yesterday, not composing a text. Just the warmth, the steam rising, the first bitter-sweet contact with the tongue. This is what every contemplative tradition means by presence — not a special state you achieve, but a return to what is already happening. The cup teaches it without explanation.

"In the tea room it is enough to cleanse the flower vases, light the charcoal and arrange the flowers, create coolness in summer and warmth in winter."
— Sen Rikyu (1522–1591), the great systematizer of the Japanese tea ceremony

The morning porch ritual is already doing this. The tea is already the practice. Naming it doesn't change what happens — it deepens your awareness of what was already there. Drink slowly. Let the cup be the whole world for a few minutes before the world arrives to ask things of you.

Choose one invocation for the morning, or none. Let the choice follow the season, your mood, what you need. Rotate freely.

Ancestors
I woke today because others endured before me — through crossings I cannot imagine, in languages I no longer speak. I carry their survival in my body. This morning, with this tea, I remember them. I am the continuation.
Trees & Living World
The trees on my street were here before I was and may outlast me. The nearest park is exhaling right now, and I am breathing what the trees give. I am not separate from this neighborhood. I am a neighbor to all of it.
Presence with Loved Ones
This person beside me, this animal against my leg — they will not always be here, and neither will I. Let me not miss this morning by thinking about the afternoon. Let me be fully where I am, with whom I am.
Deep Time
The sun that warms this porch has risen over this place for four and a half billion years. The hills I see were seafloor once. The tea in my hands grew from soil that was stone, that was magma, that was stardust. I am a brief visitor in an ancient story. That is enough.
Seasonal Awareness
What season is it — not on the calendar, but in the world? What is blooming, what is dormant, what is migrating through? Let me notice the light, the temperature, the angle of the sun. My body knows what time it is if I let it.
Reciprocity
I have already received today: warmth, breath, this cup, this company. Before the day asks anything of me, let me ask what I will give back. Not out of guilt, but because exchange is how living systems endure.
Wonder
I do not understand why anything exists at all. I do not understand consciousness, or love, or why the light at this hour turns everything gold. I do not need to understand. Let me begin this day in honest amazement.

Morning Walk

Ten to twenty minutes through your neighborhood. Notice specific trees, listen for birds, observe seasonal changes. Walk without earbuds. Let the world speak first.

If you want an intention: pick one tree on your route and learn its name. Watch it across weeks. Notice when it flowers, when it drops leaves, when birds prefer it. You are building a relationship.

Seasonal Variations

Winter. The sun rises later and lower. Your porch is cooler, the tea matters more. The hills are brown or, after rain, startlingly green. Morning practice in winter is about rest and interiority — the invocations on ancestors and deep time feel most natural now. Let the darkness have its say.

Spring. The light returns earlier each week. Trees bloom on your street. Birds are louder, more insistent. Morning practice in spring is about noticing emergence — what is growing? What is returning? The seasonal awareness and trees invocations fit here.

Summer. The sun is already up when you are. The air is warm on the porch. You may be heading soon toward your wild place — the mountains, the water, wherever restores you. Morning practice in summer is about abundance and gratitude — the reciprocity and presence invocations are strongest now.

Autumn. The light goes golden and oblique. Days shorten perceptibly. The High Holidays come. You are back from the mountains, carrying what they gave you. Morning practice in autumn is about letting go — the wonder and deep time invocations help you release what the season is releasing.

Movement

Silent Movement

One or two workouts per week in silence — no podcasts, no music with words. Just your body, your breath, the sound of your feet or the weight in your hands. This is where meditation lives for people who move.

Embodied Attention
My body is not a vehicle for my mind. It is the instrument through which I know the world. For this next hour, let me attend to what it tells me — the pull, the breath, the ground beneath my feet.
The Tracker's Eye
Boyd Varty says: "Tracking is the art of paying attention." On this run, on this court, let me track — the wind direction, the shadow length, the birds that scatter as I pass. The world is speaking. I am practicing how to listen.
Walking Meditation
Each step is an arrival, not a means to a destination. I am not going somewhere. I am here, and here, and here again. The ground receives my weight. I receive the ground.

Intentional Listening

On the workouts that do include podcasts or audiobooks, consider rotating in material from the canon: an audiobook of Meditations or Braiding Sweetgrass, a lecture on Daoism or forest ecology. Not every workout — but often enough that your commute and exercise time become a form of study.

Contemplation

Sitting

Five to ten minutes. Non-dogmatic. You can follow your breath, observe your thoughts, or simply sit in silence. The form matters less than the consistency.

A simple method, drawn from vipassana and Daoist zuowang: sit comfortably. Close your eyes or lower your gaze. Breathe naturally — do not force the breath. Place your attention on the sensation of air entering and leaving your nostrils. When your mind wanders (it will, constantly), notice that it has wandered, and return to the breath. That noticing is the practice. Each return is a repetition, like a bicep curl for attention.

You do not need to empty your mind. You do not need to achieve a state. You are training the capacity to notice where your attention is and to redirect it gently. Marcus Aurelius called this prosoche — attention. The Daoists called it zuowang — sitting and forgetting. Sam Harris calls it "the practice of recognizing what consciousness is like." It is the same skill, described in different centuries.

Study

Read from the canon. Listen to an audiobook on your commute. Engage with ideas that expand your sense of what is real and what matters. Study is spiritual practice — in the Jewish tradition, Torah study is considered equal to all other commandments combined. You do not need to be Jewish to recognize the insight: sustained, serious engagement with difficult texts changes who you are.

A Reading Path by Season

The canon is not meant to be read all at once. It is meant to be returned to, the way you return to a trail you know by heart and still notice new things. Different texts fit different seasons — not as rule, but as natural alignment. What follows is a loose guide:

Winter (December–February): Meditations by Marcus Aurelius, the Tao Te Ching, Ecclesiastes. These are the short, dense texts you can read in a weekend but spend a lifetime with. Winter is for rereading — for sitting still with what you already know and finding what you missed. The cold clarifies. The long nights accommodate Marcus's midnight mood.

Spring (March–May): Braiding Sweetgrass (Kimmerer), the Dhammapada, Pirkei Avot. Spring wants texts about gift, reciprocity, and ethical beginning — things you can take outside and test against a blooming world. Kimmerer's chapters on planting are especially suited to March and April. The Dhammapada is short enough to carry on a camping trip.

Summer (June–August): My First Summer in the Sierra (Muir), Assembling California (McPhee), the Zhuangzi. Summer is for reading in the field — on the trail, under trees, beside a lake. Muir belongs at elevation. McPhee belongs on a long drive through the desert. The Zhuangzi belongs anywhere you want to dissolve the line between thought and landscape.

Autumn (September–November): Antifragile (Taleb), Finding the Mother Tree (Simard), Tribe (Junger). Autumn is for synthesis — letting the year's experiences be read through texts that think in systems. What became stronger from stress? What did you harvest? What are you releasing? Taleb and Simard think about the same questions from opposite ends.

Nature Observation

Learn the trees in your neighborhood. Notice when the birds change. Watch the light shift through the seasons. You don't need to be in the Sierra to practice attention to the living world.

Start with what's nearest. The trees along your street. The birds in the nearest park. The hawk or crow that circles overhead. Knowing their names is the beginning of a relationship. Watching them across months is the deepening of it.

Silence & Attention

Every contemplative tradition has recognized what the technology critics of our century have rediscovered: that attention is finite, that it can be trained or squandered, and that the quality of a life is inseparable from the quality of attention that fills it. This section is about creating deliberate space for silence in a life that has almost none.

The Case for Silence

Neil Postman wrote that we are amusing ourselves to death — that the real threat is the flood of information that drowns out meaning. Cal Newport calls it "solitude deprivation" — the state of never being alone with your own thoughts. The Daoist tradition has a simpler name: noise.

The morning porch is already a space of relative quiet. But the rest of the day — podcasts during workouts, podcasts during commutes, TV in the evenings — leaves almost no room for the mind to encounter itself. Silence is where ideas consolidate. It is where grief surfaces when it is ready. It is where the insights from the canon actually take root. Without silence, study is just input.

A Digital Sabbath

Adapted from Shabbat's prohibition on work — which is really a prohibition on creating, on imposing your will on the world for one day out of seven. The digital version: one evening per week, or one morning per weekend, with no screens. No phone, no laptop, no television. The discomfort of the first twenty minutes is the practice. What comes after — boredom, restlessness, then a strange settling — is the point.

Entering Silence
For the next hour I will not be reachable, and the world will continue without my attention. The notifications can accumulate. The news will still be the news when I return. What I am doing now — sitting with my own mind, without input — is older than any technology and more necessary than any of them.

Silent Commute

Once or twice a week, drive or walk without audio. Let the commute be empty. Notice what your mind does when it is not being fed — what it returns to, what it avoids, what surfaces unbidden. The Stoics called this examining your impressions. The Buddhists call it noting. The practice is the same: let the silence show you what is actually on your mind.

The Attention Audit

Nassim Taleb observes that the modern world floods us with information precisely because most of it is noise — the signal-to-noise ratio degrades as volume increases. An attention audit is simple: at the end of any day, ask what you consumed that you will remember in a week. The answer is usually very little. The rest was noise dressed up as urgency. The canon exists to replace some of that noise with signal — texts that have survived centuries because they contain things worth returning to.

"We have become combatants in the war against our own attention. The technologies that were supposed to free us have instead become the most efficient tools ever devised for the distraction and fragmentation of human consciousness."
— adapted from Neil Postman, Technopoly (1992)
"Solitude requires you to move past reacting to information created by other people and focus instead on your own thoughts and experiences — this is a fundamental human need."
— Cal Newport, Digital Minimalism (2019)

Evening

Reflection

Five minutes before sleep. No special posture, no ceremony. Just a brief, honest accounting — a genuine noticing.

Gratitude
What did I receive today that I did not earn? A meal someone prepared. A conversation that surprised me. The particular quality of light at a particular hour. Let me name one gift before I sleep.
The Principles
Which of the eight did I embody today? Which did I neglect? No judgment — just observation. Tomorrow is another attempt. The practice is in the returning.
Impermanence
This day is over and will not come again. Whatever I did or failed to do, it is done. Let me release it as a tree releases a leaf — not in grief, but because holding on is not what living things do.
Connection
Who did I love well today? Who did I fail to see? Tomorrow, let me be more present to the people and creatures who share this life with me. They are not permanent. Neither am I.

Presence

Put your phone in another room. Be with the people and animals who share your home. This is the point.
