The Canon

Curated texts, sources, and further reading

Foundational Reading

If you read nothing else, read these. They are the closest thing Arbora has to scripture — because they articulate what we value with extraordinary clarity.

Nature & Interconnection

Robin Wall Kimmerer, Braiding Sweetgrass — Indigenous wisdom and scientific knowledge woven together. The Honorable Harvest. The grammar of animacy. This is the heart of Arbora's relationship to the living world.

Suzanne Simard, Finding the Mother Tree — The science of mycorrhizal networks. Trees communicate, share resources, and nurture their young through underground fungal connections. Interconnection as empirical fact.

Peter Wohlleben, The Hidden Life of Trees — How trees form communities, care for each other, and exhibit behaviors we might call social. Changes how you see every forest.

Philosophy & Ethics

Marcus Aurelius, Meditations — A Roman emperor's private journal of Stoic practice. How to live with clarity, virtue, and cosmic perspective. Written for himself, not for publication — which is why it feels so honest.

Lao Tzu, Tao Te Ching — 81 short chapters on the nature of reality, the art of non-striving, and harmony with the way things are. Read slowly, one chapter at a time.

Nassim Nicholas Taleb, Antifragile — Systems that gain from disorder. Applies to forests, to diaspora cultures, to evolution, to how you should live. Ostensibly secular, but deeply spiritual in its implications.

Consciousness & Presence

Sam Harris, Waking Up — Secular meditation and the nature of consciousness. A bridge between scientific materialism and genuine spiritual experience.

Robert Pirsig, Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance — Quality, attention, and the relationship between rational and romantic modes of understanding.

Deep Time & Place

John McPhee, Assembling California — Geology as narrative. The deep time of the land beneath your feet.

John Muir, My First Summer in the Sierra — Pure, ecstatic encounter with the Sierra Nevada. The first great document of the religion that would become Arbora.

Traditional Societies & Indigenous Wisdom

Jared Diamond, The World Until Yesterday — What modern societies can learn from traditional ones. Comparative anthropology as spiritual practice.

Wade Davis, The Wayfinders — Why ancient wisdom matters in the modern world. A defense of cognitive and cultural diversity.

Sherri Mitchell, Sacred Instructions — Indigenous wisdom for living spirit-based change.

Technology & Attention

Neil Postman, Technopoly — The surrender of culture to technology. Intellectual grounding for why silence and presence matter more now than ever.

Classical Philosophy

The Greco-Roman philosophical tradition is not one thing. What follows are passages from its most aligned schools — texts that have been read and reread for millennia because they articulate truths about how to live that have not expired.

Marcus Aurelius, Meditations

A Roman emperor's private notebook, never intended for publication. The most intimate document in Stoic philosophy — a man arguing with himself about how to be good.

"Constantly regard the universe as one living being, having one substance and one soul; and observe how all things have reference to one perception, the perception of this one living being; and how all things act with one movement; and how all things are the cooperating causes of all things that exist; observe too the continuous spinning of the thread and the structure of the web."
— IV.40 (trans. George Long)
"How small a part of the boundless and unfathomable time is assigned to every man! For it is very soon swallowed up in the eternal. And how small a part of the whole substance! And how small a part of the universal soul! And on what a small clod of the whole earth you creep! Reflecting on all this, consider nothing to be great, except to act as your nature leads you, and to endure that which the common nature brings."
— V.24

Epictetus, Enchiridion

A former slave who became one of the greatest Stoic teachers. The Enchiridion (Handbook) opens with the most fundamental distinction in practical philosophy.

"Some things are within our power, while others are not. Within our power are opinion, motivation, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever is of our own doing. Not within our power are our body, our property, reputation, office, and, in a word, whatever is not of our own doing."
— Chapter 1 (trans. Elizabeth Carter, adapted)

Seneca, Letters to Lucilius

Seneca wrote 124 moral letters to his friend Lucilius — part philosophy, part therapy, part the best advice you've ever received from someone who is also failing at following it.

"It is not that we have a short time to live, but that we waste a great deal of it. Life is long enough, and a sufficiently generous amount has been given to us for the highest achievements if it were all well invested. But when it is wasted in heedless luxury and spent on no good activity, we are forced at last by death's final constraint to realize that it has passed away before we knew it was passing."
— On the Shortness of Life, 1 (trans. C.D.N. Costa)

Heraclitus, Fragments

A pre-Socratic philosopher from Ephesus who wrote in enigmatic aphorisms. Only fragments survive — which somehow makes them more potent.

"No man ever steps in the same river twice, for it's not the same river and he's not the same man."
— Fragment 12 (paraphrase, widely attributed)
"The way up and the way down are one and the same."
— Fragment 60
"Nature loves to hide."
— Fragment 123

Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus

Epicureanism is the most misunderstood school. Epicureanism is the pursuit of simple pleasures, friendship, philosophical contemplation, and freedom from anxiety.

"Accustom yourself to believing that death is nothing to us, for good and evil imply the capacity for sensation, and death is the privation of all sentience... Death, therefore, the most awful of evils, is nothing to us, seeing that, when we are, death is not come, and, when death is come, we are not."
— (trans. Robert Drew Hicks)

Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics

The foundational text of Western virtue ethics. Aristotle's answer to "what is the good life?" is eudaimonia — flourishing — achieved through habitual practice of excellence.

"One swallow does not make a summer, nor does one day; and so too one day, or a short time, does not make a man blessed and happy... We become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts, brave by doing brave acts."
— Book I and Book II (trans. W.D. Ross, adapted)

Sacred Texts

Excerpts from the world's wisdom traditions. Each is presented with brief context and connection to Arbora's values. These are meant to be returned to, the way you return to a trail you know by heart and still notice new things.

Lao Tzu, Tao Te Ching

The foundational text of Daoism. On the nature of what cannot be named.

"The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao.
The name that can be named is not the eternal name.
The nameless is the beginning of heaven and earth.
The named is the mother of ten thousand things.
Ever desireless, one can see the mystery.
Ever desiring, one can see the manifestations.
These two spring from the same source but differ in name;
this appears as darkness.
Darkness within darkness.
The gate to all mystery."
— Chapter 1 (trans. Gia-Fu Feng & Jane English)
"The Tao gives birth to One.
One gives birth to Two.
Two gives birth to Three.
Three gives birth to ten thousand things.
The ten thousand things carry yin and embrace yang.
They achieve harmony by combining these forces."
— Chapter 42

Dhammapada

A collection of sayings attributed to the Buddha, from the Pali Canon. The most accessible entry point to Buddhist thought.

"We are what we think. All that we are arises with our thoughts. With our thoughts we make the world."
— Verse 1 (trans. Thomas Byrom)
"Hatred does not cease by hatred, but only by love; this is the eternal rule."
— Verse 5
"All conditioned things are impermanent. When one sees this with wisdom, one turns away from suffering. This is the path to purification."
— Verse 277

Heart Sutra

The most concentrated expression of Mahayana Buddhist philosophy. A single page that has been chanted daily across East Asia for over a millennium.

"Form is emptiness, emptiness is form. Emptiness is not other than form, form is not other than emptiness. In the same way, feeling, perception, mental formation, and consciousness are emptiness... There is no suffering, no origin of suffering, no cessation of suffering, no path. There is no wisdom and no attainment."
— (trans. Thich Nhat Hanh, adapted)

Kalama Sutta

The Buddha's instruction on critical thinking — arguably the most "modern" passage in the Pali Canon, and the source of Arbora's seventh principle.

"Do not go upon what has been acquired by repeated hearing; nor upon tradition; nor upon rumor; nor upon what is in a scripture; nor upon surmise; nor upon an axiom; nor upon specious reasoning; nor upon a bias toward a notion that has been pondered over; nor upon another's seeming ability; nor upon the consideration 'The monk is our teacher.' When you yourselves know: 'These things are good; these things are not blamable; these things are praised by the wise; undertaken and observed, these things lead to benefit and happiness,' enter on and abide in them."
— Anguttara Nikaya 3.65 (trans. Soma Thera, adapted)

Pirkei Avot (Ethics of the Fathers)

A tractate of the Mishnah — the oldest layer of rabbinic literature. Not law but wisdom. These are the things Jewish teachers said to each other across centuries about what matters.

"If I am not for myself, who will be for me? But if I am only for myself, what am I? And if not now, when?"
— Hillel the Elder, 1:14
"It is not upon you to finish the work, but neither are you free to desist from it."
— Rabbi Tarfon, 2:16
"Who is wise? One who learns from every person. Who is mighty? One who subdues their own inclination. Who is rich? One who is happy with their lot. Who is honored? One who honors others."
— Ben Zoma, 4:1

Zhuangzi

The second great Daoist text, after the Tao Te Ching. Where Laozi is concise, Zhuangzi is wild, funny, and subversive. His philosophy dissolves categories.

"Once Zhuangzi dreamt he was a butterfly, a butterfly flitting and fluttering around, happy with himself and doing as he pleased. He didn't know he was Zhuangzi. Suddenly he woke up and there he was, solid and unmistakable Zhuangzi. But he didn't know if he was Zhuangzi who had dreamt he was a butterfly, or a butterfly dreaming he was Zhuangzi."
— Chapter 2, "Discussion on Making All Things Equal" (trans. Burton Watson, adapted)

Chandogya Upanishad

One of the oldest Upanishads, from the Hindu philosophical tradition. Contains the teaching of non-duality that echoes across Buddhist, Daoist, and mystical traditions worldwide.

"In the beginning there was Existence alone — One only, without a second. It thought to itself: 'Let me become many, let me grow forth.' Thus it projected the universe... That which is the finest essence — this whole world has that as its soul. That is Reality. That is the Self. Tat tvam asi — Thou art that."
— 6.2–6.12 (trans. Eknath Easwaran, adapted)

Rumi

Jalal ad-Din Muhammad Rumi, 13th-century Sufi poet. His poetry dissolves the boundary between sacred and profane, self and beloved, human and divine.

"This being human is a guest house.
Every morning a new arrival.
A joy, a depression, a meanness,
some momentary awareness comes
as an unexpected visitor.
Welcome and entertain them all!
Even if they're a crowd of sorrows,
who violently sweep your house
empty of its furniture,
still, treat each guest honorably.
He may be clearing you out
for some new delight."
— "The Guest House" (trans. Coleman Barks)
"Out beyond ideas of wrongdoing and rightdoing,
there is a field. I'll meet you there.
When the soul lies down in that grass,
the world is too full to talk about.
Ideas, language, even the phrase 'each other'
doesn't make any sense."
— (trans. Coleman Barks)

Francis of Assisi, Canticle of the Sun

Written around 1225, this is the oldest surviving work of Italian literature — and a hymn to the living world that needs no theology to be felt. Read "Praised" as an expression of gratitude.

"Praised be You, my Lord, through Brother Sun,
who brings the day; and You give light through him.
And he is beautiful and radiant in all his splendor!
Of You, Most High, he bears the likeness.

Praised be You, my Lord, through Sister Moon and the stars;
in heaven You formed them clear and precious and beautiful.

Praised be You, my Lord, through Sister Water,
who is very useful and humble and precious and chaste.

Praised be You, my Lord, through Brother Fire,
through whom You light the night,
and he is beautiful and playful and robust and strong."
— (trans. Bill Barrett, adapted)

Poetry

Poetry does what philosophy cannot: it bypasses the arguing mind and lands in the body. These poems are chosen because they enact Arbora's values rather than merely describing them.

Matsuo Basho

The great haiku master. Three lines, seventeen syllables, the whole world.

"The old pond —
a frog jumps in,
sound of water."
"Even in Kyoto —
hearing the cuckoo's cry —
I long for Kyoto."
"Nothing in the cry
of cicadas suggests
they are about to die."

Mary Oliver, "The Summer Day"

The closing lines of this poem are as close to a creed as Arbora has.

"I don't know exactly what a prayer is.
I do know how to pay attention, how to fall down
into the grass, how to kneel down in the grass,
how to be idle and blessed, how to stroll through the fields,
which is what I have been doing all day.
Tell me, what else should I have done?
Doesn't everything die at last, and too soon?
Tell me, what is it you plan to do
with your one wild and precious life?"

Wendell Berry, "The Peace of Wild Things"

On what the living world offers when anxiety overwhelms.

"When despair for the world grows in me
and I wake in the night at the least sound
in fear of what my life and my children's lives may be,
I go and lie down where the wood drake
rests in his beauty on the water, and the great heron feeds.
I come into the peace of wild things
who do not tax their lives with forethought
of grief. I come into the presence of still water.
And I feel above me the day-blind stars
waiting with their light. For a time
I rest in the grace of the world, and am free."

Gary Snyder, from "For the Children"

The poet of wild mind and ecological consciousness. This is deep time as parenting advice.

"stay together
learn the flowers
go light"

Robinson Jeffers, from "Carmel Point"

Jeffers' philosophy of "inhumanism" — the recognition that beauty and meaning exist beyond the human scale.

"As for us:
We must uncenter our minds from ourselves;
We must unhumanize our views a little, and become confident
As the rock and ocean that we were made from."

Yehuda Amichai, "The Place Where We Are Right"

Israel's greatest modern poet. This poem is Arbora's seventh principle — question everything — rendered as image.

"From the place where we are right
flowers will never grow
in the spring.

The place where we are right
is hard and trampled
like a yard.

But doubts and loves
dig up the world
like a mole, a plow.
And a whisper will be heard in the place
where the ruined
house once stood."
— (trans. Stephen Mitchell)

Kabir

A 15th-century Indian mystic poet who belonged to no religion and all of them. He wove Hindu and Muslim imagery into poems of radical non-duality.

"Are you looking for me? I am in the next seat.
My shoulder is against yours.
You will not find me in stupas, not in Indian shrine rooms,
nor in synagogues, nor in cathedrals:
not in masses, nor kirtans, not in legs winding
around your own neck, nor in eating nothing but vegetables.
When you really look for me, you will see me instantly —
you will find me in the tiniest house of time.
Kabir says: Student, tell me, what is God?
He is the breath inside the breath."
— (trans. Robert Bly)

Prayers & Invocations

These are adapted from existing traditions — Jewish brachot, Buddhist chants, indigenous prayers, Daoist liturgy — but stripped of theistic framing. They address the world, the community, the ancestors, or the self. Use them as written, adapt them, or let them inspire your own. The form is a gift from the traditions; the words are yours to shape.

Morning

I woke today. This is not guaranteed and never was. My ancestors — in Grodno, in Vilna, in every place of passage and peril — woke on mornings far harder than this and chose to continue. I continue in their name. Let me use this day with the seriousness it deserves.
— Adapted from the Jewish Modeh Ani (morning gratitude prayer)
Waking, I vow with all beings to be grateful for each moment and to attend to it fully — not rehearsing the past, not drafting the future, but arriving here.
— Adapted from Robert Aitken's Buddhist morning verse

Meals

We give thanks for this food — for the soil that grew it, the sun and rain that fed it, the hands that harvested and prepared it. We eat not alone but together, and in doing so we remember that every meal connects us to the living world and to each other.
— Adapted from the Jewish hamotzi and indigenous gratitude practices
This food is the gift of the whole universe — the earth, the sky, and much hard work. May we eat in mindfulness so as to be worthy to receive it. May we transform our unskillful states of mind and learn to eat with moderation. May we take only foods that nourish us and prevent illness. We accept this food to realize the path of understanding and love.
— Adapted from Thich Nhat Hanh's Five Contemplations

Seasonal

The light is returning. What was dormant stirs. I align myself with the season of growth — not forcing, but making conditions for emergence. Let what needs to begin, begin.
— For the light half of the year (spring equinox through autumn equinox)
The dark is deepening. The world rests, and I may rest too. I align myself with the season of inwardness — reading, reflection, the slow work of roots beneath frozen ground. What endures does not need the light to endure.
— For the dark half of the year (autumn equinox through spring equinox)

Community Gathering

We gather at this table not out of obligation but out of the oldest human instinct: the need to be together, to share food, to speak honestly, and to know that we are not alone. For the next few hours, let us be fully here — not performing, not producing, not elsewhere in our minds. This is the practice. This is the point.
— Opening blessing for the weekly gathering
We came from separate days and separate concerns. For a while we were together, and that togetherness was its own kind of sustenance. We leave now carrying something we did not have when we arrived. Go gently. Come back.
— Closing blessing for the weekly gathering

Ancestor Remembrance

We remember those who are no longer at this table but whose presence made this table possible. We name them not to summon them but to honor the truth that we are the continuation of their lives, their choices, their endurance. They crossed oceans, survived winters, buried children, and kept going — so that, eventually, improbably, we could be here. We do not let them become abstractions. We say their names. We tell their stories. We live in a way that would make the journey worth it.
— Adapted from the Jewish Yizkor and Kaddish traditions

Wilderness

I enter this place as a guest — not an owner, not a conqueror, not a consumer. The trees were here before me. The stone beneath the trail is older than my species. The water I will drink tonight has been cycling through rock and sky for longer than I can comprehend. Let me walk with the attention this place deserves. Let me take only what I need and leave more than I found. Let me be worthy of the silence.
— For arriving in the mountains

Ancestral & Historical Texts

The religion of interconnection includes connection backward through time — to the specific people who made your existence possible. These texts speak to exile, survival, cultural persistence, and the long memory of peoples who endured. They are here because ancestry is not abstract. It is particular: specific names, specific towns, specific crossings.

Simon Dubnow, History of the Jews

The great historian of the Jewish diaspora. Dubnow understood Jewish history as a story of cultural survival without sovereignty — a people who maintained identity through text, law, community, and sheer persistence across two millennia of dispersion.

"The Jews have survived the mightiest empires of antiquity — Egypt, Assyria, Babylon, Greece, Rome — because they possessed an inner strength that did not depend on territory or political power, but on the life of the spirit and the solidarity of the community."
— Simon Dubnow, History of the Jews (paraphrased from multiple passages)

Primo Levi, If This Is a Man (Survival in Auschwitz)

A chemist from Turin who survived Auschwitz and wrote about it with devastating precision. Levi's work is a testament to the human capacity for observation and dignity under conditions designed to destroy both.

"Consider if this is a man,
Who labours in the mud,
Who knows no peace,
Who fights for a crust of bread,
Who dies at a yes or a no.
Consider if this is a woman,
Without hair or name,
With no more strength to remember,
Empty eyes and cold womb
Like a frog in winter.
Consider that this has been:
I commend these words to you.
Engrave them on your hearts
When you are in your house, when you walk on your way,
When you go to bed, when you rise.
Repeat them to your children."
— Primo Levi, epigraph to If This Is a Man (1947)

Yehuda Amichai, "Jews in the Land of Israel"

Israel's greatest modern poet wrote about identity, displacement, and memory with an intimacy that makes the political feel personal and the historical feel immediate.

"Jews in the Land of Israel...we forget where we came from. Our Jewish names from the Exile give us away, bring back the memory of flower and fruit, medieval cities, metals, knights who turned to feedback, the yearning of ships' masts for distance, spice boxes, gold, scarlet thread."
— Yehuda Amichai (trans. Benjamin and Barbara Harshav)

S. Ansky, The Dybbuk

A play written in Yiddish in 1914, set in a Hasidic community. It captures the mystical, ecstatic dimension of Eastern European Jewish life — the world of Adam's ancestors in Grodno and Vilna — with its belief in the permeability of the boundary between living and dead.

"Not only are the dead among the living, but the living are among the dead. The whole world is one great chain, and every link connects to every other."
— S. Ansky, The Dybbuk (1914, adapted)

Frederick Douglass, Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass

Ancestry and survival are not only Jewish themes. Douglass's account of enslaving and liberation is one of the most powerful documents of human endurance in the American tradition — and a reminder that the soil beneath your feet holds stories of suffering and resistance that predate your own.

"I have often been utterly astonished, since I came to the north, to find persons who could speak of the singing, among slaves, as evidence of their contentment and happiness. It is impossible to conceive of a greater mistake. Slaves sing most when they are most unhappy. The songs of the slave represent the sorrows of his heart; and he is relieved by them, only as an aching heart is relieved by its tears."
— Frederick Douglass, Narrative (1845)

Czeslaw Milosz, "Dedication" from Rescue (1945)

Written in Warsaw in 1945, addressed to the dead. Milosz — a Polish Nobel laureate — understood that the survivor's task is not merely to remember, but to speak for those who no longer can.

"What is poetry which does not save
Nations or people?
A connivance with official lies,
A song of drunkards whose throats will be cut in a moment,
Readings for sophomore girls.
That I wanted good poetry without knowing it,
That I discovered, late, its survey purpose,
In this and only this I find salvation."
— Czeslaw Milosz, "Dedication" (trans. the author)

Scientific & Natural History

Science, practiced with attention and humility, is a form of revelation. These texts render the deep time, interconnection, and intelligence of the natural world in language that makes empirical fact feel like sacred story. They are scripture for those who find the universe more astonishing the more precisely they look at it.

Suzanne Simard, Finding the Mother Tree

A forest ecologist whose decades of research proved that trees communicate and share resources through underground mycorrhizal networks. Her work gave scientific language to what indigenous peoples have always known: the forest is a community.

"The Mother Trees were the biggest, oldest trees in the forest, and they were connected to all the other trees around them through the mycorrhizal network. They were sending carbon and nutrients and water to the younger trees. They were nurturing their offspring, and they were sharing information about danger. The forest was behaving as though it were a single organism."
— Suzanne Simard, Finding the Mother Tree (2021)

Peter Wohlleben, The Hidden Life of Trees

A German forester who changed how millions of people see forests — as societies rather than collections of individual organisms.

"When trees grow together, nutrients and water can be shared through the underground network. This means that individual trees do not simply compete with each other. They work together. A tree is not a tree in isolation. It is a member of a community... Trees in a forest care for each other, sometimes even going so far as to nourish the stump of a felled tree for centuries after it was cut down."
— Peter Wohlleben, The Hidden Life of Trees (2015)

John McPhee, Annals of the Former World

The greatest prose stylist to write about geology. McPhee makes deep time feel like narrative — vast, humbling, and strangely personal. His four-volume journey across North America along Interstate 80 reads the continent like a text.

"The summit of Mt. Everest is marine limestone. This is the kind of observation that makes geologists rapt and a little bit wild. If you were to go to the top of Mt. Everest, you would find the fossilized remains of sea creatures in the rock beneath your feet. The Himalayas are the seafloor, lifted seven miles into the air."
— John McPhee, Annals of the Former World (1998)
"In geologic time, a human lifetime is reduced to a brevity that is too inhibiting to think about deep in the night... A day is a millionth of a human lifetime, but it is a billionth of a river's."
— John McPhee, Basin and Range

Stefano Mancuso, The Revolutionary Genius of Plants

An Italian plant neurobiologist who argues that plants are intelligent — that they solve problems, communicate, make decisions, and exhibit behavior that, in animals, we would not hesitate to call cognition.

"Plants are able to sense and calculate their environment with a precision that is the envy of the most gifted animals. They can sense gravity, electromagnetic fields, humidity, light, chemicals, temperature gradients, and even sounds. They make decisions. The root system of a single plant may make millions of computations per second as it navigates through soil."
— Stefano Mancuso, The Revolutionary Genius of Plants (2017)

Charles Darwin, On the Origin of Species

The closing paragraph of the Origin — one of the most beautiful passages in the history of science, and a statement of interconnection and wonder that belongs in any canon of nature reverence.

"There is grandeur in this view of life, with its several powers, having been originally breathed into a few forms or into one; and that, whilst this planet has gone cycling on according to the fixed law of gravity, from so simple a beginning endless forms most beautiful and most wonderful have been, and are being, evolved."
— Charles Darwin, On the Origin of Species (1859)

Rachel Carson, Silent Spring

The book that launched the modern environmental movement. Carson — a marine biologist with a poet's ear — documented the devastation caused by pesticides with such clarity and moral force that the world could not look away.

"In nature nothing exists alone. Every living thing exists in relation to every other living thing — the web of life in which there is no single strand that can be touched without the vibration being felt throughout the entire web."
— Rachel Carson, Silent Spring (1962)
