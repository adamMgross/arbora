Ancestral & Historical Texts

The religion of interconnection includes connection backward through time — to the specific people who made your existence possible. These texts speak to exile, survival, cultural persistence, and the long memory of peoples who endured. They are here because ancestry is not abstract. It is particular: specific names, specific towns, specific crossings.

Simon Dubnow, History of the Jews

The great historian of the Jewish diaspora. Dubnow understood Jewish history as a story of cultural survival without sovereignty — a people who maintained identity through text, law, community, and sheer persistence across two millennia of dispersion.

"The Jews have survived the mightiest empires of antiquity — Egypt, Assyria, Babylon, Greece, Rome — because they possessed an inner strength that did not depend on territory or political power, but on the life of the spirit and the solidarity of the community."
— Simon Dubnow, History of the Jews (paraphrased from multiple passages)

Abraham Joshua Heschel

Heschel (1907–1972) was born in Warsaw into a dynasty of Hasidic rabbis, received a doctorate in Berlin, and emigrated to America in 1940 — weeks before the Nazis would have killed him. His mother and three sisters died in the Holocaust. He became one of the most important Jewish theologians of the 20th century, and marched beside Martin Luther King Jr. in Selma in 1965. His theology is not systematic — it is phenomenological. He wrote about the experience of being alive as inherently sacred, and about wonder as the beginning of all genuine spiritual life.

"Just to be is a blessing. Just to live is holy."
— Abraham Joshua Heschel, Man Is Not Alone (1951)
"Life is routine and routine is resistance to wonder."
— Abraham Joshua Heschel, Man Is Not Alone
"Wonder or radical amazement is the chief characteristic of the religious man's attitude toward history and nature. One attitude is alien to his spirit: taking things for granted, regarding events as a natural course of things."
— Abraham Joshua Heschel, God in Search of Man (1955)

Primo Levi, If This Is a Man (Survival in Auschwitz)

A chemist from Turin who survived Auschwitz and wrote about it with devastating precision. Levi's work is a testament to the human capacity for observation and dignity under conditions designed to destroy both.

"Consider if this is a man,
Who labours in the mud,
Who knows no peace,
Who fights for a crust of bread,
Who dies at a yes or a no.
Consider if this is a woman,
Without hair or name,
With no more strength to remember,
Empty eyes and cold womb
Like a frog in winter.
Consider that this has been:
I commend these words to you.
Engrave them on your hearts
When you are in your house, when you walk on your way,
When you go to bed, when you rise.
Repeat them to your children."
— Primo Levi, epigraph to If This Is a Man (1947)

Paul Celan, "Death Fugue" (Todesfuge)

Paul Celan (1920–1970) was a Romanian Jewish poet who wrote in German — the language of his parents' killers. His mother and father died in the Nazi camps; he survived forced labor. "Death Fugue," written in 1944–45, is among the most powerful poems in any language about the Shoah. It achieves its effect through repetition and rhythm, using the formal structure of a fugue to enact the horror it describes. Celan spent decades revising his translations of it into French and English, never satisfied — the poem was both inescapable and impossible.

"Black milk of daybreak we drink it at evening
we drink it at midday and morning we drink it at night
we drink and we drink
we shovel a grave in the air there you won't lie too cramped
A man lives in the house he plays with the serpents he writes
he writes when dusk falls to Germany your golden hair Margarete
your ashen hair Shulamith we shovel a grave in the air there
you won't lie too cramped."
— Paul Celan, "Death Fugue" (trans. Michael Hamburger)

Sholem Aleichem

Sholem Aleichem (Sholem Naumovich Rabinowitz, 1859–1916) is the great Yiddish writer — the Mark Twain of Jewish literature, though the comparison undersells him. His Tevye the Dairyman stories are set in the Pale of Settlement, in the exact geographic and cultural world of Adam's Grodno and Vilna ancestors. Tevye argues with God constantly — not from doubt but from intimacy. He quotes scripture, misquotes it, improvises it to fit his situation. The tradition of arguing with God, of kvetching at the divine, is not disrespect — it is a form of the deepest engagement. You do not argue with what you do not believe in.

"Lord of the Universe, it is revealed and known before You that if You had a little spare time and came down to our world to see how Your children the Jews are getting along, You would be appalled... Help us! Help us — for if You don't, who will?"
— Sholem Aleichem, Tevye the Dairyman (adapted)
"No matter what happens to you, keep going. You are a Jew, which means you have practice."
— Sholem Aleichem (widely attributed, in the spirit of Tevye)

Yehuda Amichai, "Jews in the Land of Israel"

Israel's greatest modern poet wrote about identity, displacement, and memory with an intimacy that makes the political feel personal and the historical feel immediate.

"Jews in the Land of Israel...we forget where we came from. Our Jewish names from the Exile give us away, bring back the memory of flower and fruit, medieval cities, metals, knights who turned to feedback, the yearning of ships' masts for distance, spice boxes, gold, scarlet thread."
— Yehuda Amichai (trans. Benjamin and Barbara Harshav)

S. Ansky, The Dybbuk

A play written in Yiddish in 1914, set in a Hasidic community. It captures the mystical, ecstatic dimension of Eastern European Jewish life — the world of Eastern European Jewish life — shtetls and study houses, mystical transport and communal survival — with its belief in the permeability of the boundary between living and dead.

"Not only are the dead among the living, but the living are among the dead. The whole world is one great chain, and every link connects to every other."
— S. Ansky, The Dybbuk (1914, adapted)

Frederick Douglass, Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass

Ancestry and survival are not only Jewish themes. Douglass's account of enslaving and liberation is one of the most powerful documents of human endurance in the American tradition — and a reminder that the soil beneath your feet holds stories of suffering and resistance that predate your own.

"I have often been utterly astonished, since I came to the north, to find persons who could speak of the singing, among slaves, as evidence of their contentment and happiness. It is impossible to conceive of a greater mistake. Slaves sing most when they are most unhappy. The songs of the slave represent the sorrows of his heart; and he is relieved by them, only as an aching heart is relieved by its tears."
— Frederick Douglass, Narrative (1845)

Czeslaw Milosz, "Dedication" from Rescue (1945)

Written in Warsaw in 1945, addressed to the dead. Milosz — a Polish Nobel laureate — understood that the survivor's task is not merely to remember, but to speak for those who no longer can.

"What is poetry which does not save
Nations or people?
A connivance with official lies,
A song of drunkards whose throats will be cut in a moment,
Readings for sophomore girls.
That I wanted good poetry without knowing it,
That I discovered, late, its survey purpose,
In this and only this I find salvation."
— Czeslaw Milosz, "Dedication" (trans. the author)
