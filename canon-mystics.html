These are voices that cannot be contained by the doctrines they nominally inhabit. A Sufi poet, a Hindu-Muslim weaver, a German Dominican friar, and a 13th-century Italian saint — each speaking from direct experience of something that dissolves the boundary between self and world, between the sacred and the ordinary. They belong together because they are all saying the same thing from different angles: the separateness you feel is not the deepest truth about you.

Rumi

Jalal ad-Din Muhammad Rumi (1207–1273) was a Persian-language poet, Islamic jurist, and Sufi mystic. Born in what is now Afghanistan, he eventually settled in Konya (in present-day Turkey), where he founded the Mevlevi Order — the "whirling dervishes." The death of his friend and spiritual teacher Shams-i-Tabriz in 1248 shattered him and unleashed an outpouring of poetry that continued for the rest of his life. His Masnavi ("Spiritual Couplets") runs to 25,000 verses; the Divan-e Shams is immense. Rumi is one of the most widely read poets in the world, and in translation — particularly Coleman Barks's free renderings — he has become a presence in contemporary American culture. Use him for the feeling, not the doctrine.

"This being human is a guest house.
Every morning a new arrival.
A joy, a depression, a meanness,
some momentary awareness comes
as an unexpected visitor.
Welcome and entertain them all!
Even if they're a crowd of sorrows,
who violently sweep your house
empty of its furniture,
still, treat each guest honorably.
He may be clearing you out
for some new delight."
— "The Guest House" (trans. Coleman Barks)
"Out beyond ideas of wrongdoing and rightdoing,
there is a field. I'll meet you there.
When the soul lies down in that grass,
the world is too full to talk about.
Ideas, language, even the phrase 'each other'
doesn't make any sense."
— (trans. Coleman Barks)
"The wound is the place where the Light enters you."
— (attributed, trans. Coleman Barks)
"You were born with wings, why prefer to crawl through life?"
— (attributed)

Hafiz

Hafiz (c. 1315–1390) was a Persian poet from Shiraz whose collected poems, the Divan-e Hafez, is the most widely read book in Persian-speaking homes — more than the Quran. His poetry is formally strict (all ghazals, a demanding form) and spiritually promiscuous: wine, the beloved, the tavern are all spiritual metaphors, but they are also just wine, the beloved, and the tavern. He is funny, warm, and radically generous. The translations by Daniel Ladinsky are free adaptations rather than literal renderings, but they capture something essential about his spirit.

"Even after all this time the sun never says to the earth,
'You owe me.'
Look what happens with a love like that —
it lights the whole sky."
— (trans. Daniel Ladinsky)
"The small man builds cages for everyone he knows.
While the sage, who has to duck his head when the moon is low,
keeps dropping keys all night long
for the beautiful rowdy prisoners."
— (trans. Daniel Ladinsky)
"I have a thousand brilliant lies for the question: How are you?
I have a thousand brilliant lies for the question: What is God?
If you think that the Truth can be known from words,
if you think that the Sun and the Ocean can pass through that tiny opening called the mouth,
O someone should start laughing! Someone should start wildly laughing now!"
— (trans. Daniel Ladinsky)

Kabir

Kabir (c. 1440–1518) was a weaver from Varanasi who became one of the most beloved mystical poets in the Indian tradition. He belonged to no religion and all of them — born into a Muslim family, deeply influenced by Hindu Bhakti devotionalism, and equally at home quoting the Quran and the Upanishads. He rejected caste, ritual, and religious exclusivity with a directness that offended everyone and inspired generations. His poems survive largely through oral tradition; the texts are unstable, contested, and alive. Translations by Robert Bly are available in English.

"Are you looking for me? I am in the next seat.
My shoulder is against yours.
You will not find me in stupas, not in Indian shrine rooms,
nor in synagogues, nor in cathedrals:
not in masses, nor kirtans, not in legs winding
around your own neck, nor in eating nothing but vegetables.
When you really look for me, you will see me instantly —
you will find me in the tiniest house of time.
Kabir says: Student, tell me, what is God?
He is the breath inside the breath."
— (trans. Robert Bly)
"I have been thinking of the difference between water and the waves on it. Rising, water's still water, falling back, it is water, will you give me a hint how to tell them apart?
Because someone has made up the word 'wave,' do I have to distinguish it from water?
There is a Secret One inside us; the planets in all the galaxies pass through his hands like beads."
— (trans. Robert Bly)

Meister Eckhart

Johannes "Meister" Eckhart (c. 1260–1328) was a German Dominican friar, theologian, and mystic who pushed Christian theology toward non-duality with a boldness that alarmed the Church. He was tried for heresy near the end of his life — a charge he denied — and died before the verdict was issued. His sermons and treatises describe the soul's union with God in terms that echo Vedanta, Sufism, and Zen. The word "God" in Eckhart should be understood as the ground of being rather than a personal deity. He is one of the most penetrating thinkers about the nature of consciousness and the self in the Western tradition.

"If the only prayer you ever say in your entire life is 'thank you,' that will be enough."
— (attributed to Meister Eckhart)
"The eye through which I see God is the same eye through which God sees me; my eye and God's eye are one eye, one seeing, one knowing, one love."
— Sermons (trans. Raymond Blakney)
"We are all meant to be mothers of God. What good is it to me if this eternal birth of the divine Son takes place unceasingly but does not take place within myself?"
— Sermons
"The most powerful prayer, one well-nigh omnipotent, and the worthiest work of all is the outcome of a quiet mind. The quieter it is, the more powerful, the worthier, the deeper, the more telling and more perfect the prayer is."
— Sermons

Francis of Assisi, Canticle of the Sun

Francesco d'Assisi (1181–1226) was the son of a wealthy cloth merchant who gave away his possessions, rebuilt a ruined chapel stone by stone, and founded a religious order centered on radical poverty and love for all created things. The Canticle of the Sun, written around 1225 near the end of his life, is the oldest surviving work of Italian literature. It is addressed to the sun, the moon, the wind, the water, fire, and the earth — each praised as a brother or sister, each recognized as a fellow creature in the family of the living world. It is the most ecologically literate text in the Christian tradition. Read "Praised be" as gratitude addressed to existence itself.

"Praised be You, my Lord, through Brother Sun,
who brings the day; and You give light through him.
And he is beautiful and radiant in all his splendor!
Of You, Most High, he bears the likeness.

Praised be You, my Lord, through Sister Moon and the stars;
in heaven You formed them clear and precious and beautiful.

Praised be You, my Lord, through Brother Wind
and through the air, cloudy and serene,
and every kind of weather through which You give sustenance to Your creatures.

Praised be You, my Lord, through Sister Water,
who is very useful and humble and precious and chaste.

Praised be You, my Lord, through Brother Fire,
through whom You light the night,
and he is beautiful and playful and robust and strong.

Praised be You, my Lord, through our Sister, Mother Earth,
who sustains and governs us,
and who produces varied fruits with colored flowers and herbs."
— Canticle of the Sun (c. 1225, trans. Bill Barrett, adapted)
